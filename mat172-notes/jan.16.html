Unit Circle & Trigonometric Functions

Date: January 16, 2026

BIG IDEA

Mastering the first quadrant of the unit circle allows for the derivation of all trigonometric values across the Cartesian plane through reflections and reference angles. Understanding the relationship between arc length, radians, and the six trigonometric ratios is fundamental to pre-calculus.

KEY TERMS

Term Definition
Unit Circle A circle with a radius of $r = 1$ centered at the origin $(0,0)$.
Co-terminal Angles Two angles that share the same terminal side but differ by integer multiples of $360^\circ$ or $2\pi$ radians.
Reference Angle The acute angle formed between the terminal side of an angle and the x-axis.
Radian The angle formed when the arc length of a circle is equal to its radius (approx. $57^\circ$).
SOH-CAH-TOA Mnemonic for the ratios: $\sin = \text{opp/hyp}$, $\cos = \text{adj/hyp}$, $\tan = \text{opp/adj}$.

RULES & CALCULATIONS

  • Standard Position: Angles are measured starting from the positive x-axis, rotating counterclockwise for positive values.
  • Arc Length Formula: $s = r\theta$, where $\theta$ must be in radians.
  • Degree to Radian Conversion: Multiply by $\frac{\pi}{180^\circ}$.
  • Trigonometric Reciprocals:
    • $\csc(\theta) = \frac{1}{\sin(\theta)}$
    • $\sec(\theta) = \frac{1}{\cos(\theta)}$
    • $\cot(\theta) = \frac{1}{\tan(\theta)}$
  • Tangent Identity: $\tan(\theta) = \frac{\sin(\theta)}{\cos(\theta)}$

PROCESS: FINDING CO-TERMINAL ANGLES

  1. Identify Rotation: Determine if the angle is greater than $2\pi$ (or $360^\circ$) or less than $0$.
  2. Convert to Mixed Number: For radians, convert improper fractions (e.g., $\frac{17\pi}{3}$) to mixed numbers ($5\frac{2}{3}\pi$) to visualize revolutions.
  3. Add/Subtract Revolutions: Subtract $2\pi$ (or $360^\circ$) repeatedly until the angle falls within the interval $[0, 2\pi)$.
  4. Final Check: Ensure the final name is in the required format (fraction vs. decimal).

EXAMPLE / DEMONSTRATION

1. Finding Co-terminal for $\frac{16\pi}{3}$
Convert to mixed number: $16 \div 3 = 5\frac{1}{3}\pi$.
This represents two full rotations ($2\pi + 2\pi = 4\pi$) plus $1\frac{1}{3}\pi$.
$1\frac{1}{3}\pi = \frac{4\pi}{3}$.
Location: Quadrant III.
2. Arc Length Calculation
Find arc length $s$ for $r = 6$ inches and $\theta = 45^\circ$.
Step 1: Convert $45^\circ$ to radians $\rightarrow 45 \cdot \frac{\pi}{180} = \frac{\pi}{4}$.
Step 2: Apply formula $s = r\theta \rightarrow s = 6 \cdot \frac{\pi}{4} = \frac{3\pi}{2}$ inches.
Approximate Value: $\approx 4.71$ inches.
3. SOH-CAH-TOA in Quadrant I
Given a point $(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}, \frac{1}{2})$ on the unit circle ($r=1$):
$\sin(t) = \text{vertical} = \frac{1}{2}$
$\cos(t) = \text{horizontal} = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$
$\tan(t) = \frac{1/2}{\sqrt{3}/2} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{3}$

PRACTICE PROBLEMS

  • Find a positive co-terminal angle for $-\frac{\pi}{15}$ between $0$ and $2\pi$. ($\frac{29\pi}{15}$)
  • If the radius of a circle is $10$ cm, what is the arc length intercepted by a $60^\circ$ angle? ($\frac{10\pi}{3} \approx 10.47$ cm)
  • Find the co-terminal angle for $400^\circ$ within one rotation. ($40^\circ$)

SUMMARY

The unit circle simplifies trigonometry by setting the hypotenuse to $1$, making $\sin(\theta) = y$ and $\cos(\theta) = x$. When dealing with real-world circles where $r \neq 1$, we use $s = r\theta$ and general ratios. Always ensure your calculator is in the correct mode (Degrees vs. Radians) and remember that $\pi$ is a numerical value ($\approx 3.14159$), not just a label for an angle.
