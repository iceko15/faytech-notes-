Comprehensive Anatomy & Physiology Review

Section 1: BIG IDEA

This review focuses on the fundamental biological mechanisms that maintain life, ranging from cellular reproduction and metabolism to the regulation of the body's internal environment through homeostasis. The instructor emphasizes that understanding the relationship between structure (anatomy) and function (physiology) across different systems—nervous, skeletal, and integumentary—is critical for mastering the upcoming examination.

Section 2: KEY TERMS/CONCEPTS

Term Definition/Function
Homeostasis The body's ability to maintain a stable internal environment despite external changes.
Anabolic Metabolic pathways that build or synthesize larger molecules from smaller ones.
Catabolic Metabolic pathways that break down larger molecules into smaller ones, releasing energy.
Mitosis Cell division resulting in two identical daughter cells with 46 chromosomes each.
Meiosis Specialized cell division for sex cells resulting in 23 chromosomes.
Osteoclast A cell that breaks down bone tissue.
Osteoblast A cell that builds new bone tissue.
Meninges Protective layers of the brain/spinal cord; the Dura Mater is the "tough" outer layer.

Section 3: STEPS/PROCESS

Cellular Respiration Flow:

  1. Glycolysis: Glucose is broken down into two pyruvate molecules.
  2. Pyruvate Oxidation: Pyruvate is converted into Acetyl CoA.
  3. Krebs Cycle: A series of reactions that generate high-energy electron carriers.
  4. Electron Transport Chain (ETC): Uses ATP Synthase to produce the bulk of the cell's ATP (energy).

Section 4: RULES/IMPORTANT NOTES

  • Transcription vs. Translation: Transcription occurs first (in the nucleus), followed by Translation (in the cytoplasm).
  • Negative Feedback: The most common feedback mechanism; it works to bring the body back toward a "set point" (e.g., temperature regulation).
  • Positive Feedback: Makes a reaction greater/amplifies it (e.g., childbirth/contractions).
  • pH Relationships:
    • High H+ concentration = Acidic = Low pH number (0–6).
    • High OH- concentration = Basic = High pH number (8–14).
  • Serous Membranes: The Visceral layer touches the organ; the Parietal layer lines the cavity.

Section 5: EXAMPLE/DEMONSTRATION

Example 1: The pH Scale and Ions
If a solution has a pH of 2 (like stomach acid), it is considered highly acidic. This means it has a high concentration of Hydrogen ions (H+) and a low concentration of Hydroxide ions (OH-).
Example 2: Set Points in Homeostasis
The human body "set point" for temperature is 98.6°F. While a normal range exists (e.g., 97.5°F to 100°F), once the temperature fluctuates outside this range, the body triggers negative feedback loops to sweat or shiver to return to the set point.
Example 3: Hierarchical Organization
The body is organized from simplest to most complex: Chemicals → Cells → Tissues → Organs → Organ Systems → Organism.

Section 6: PRACTICE PROBLEMS

  1. If a cell undergoes mitosis, how many chromosomes will the new daughter cell have? (Answer: 46)
  2. Which division of the nervous system is responsible for the "fight or flight" response when being chased by a bear? (Answer: Sympathetic)
  3. What is the function of the Mitochondria? (Answer: Powerhouse of the cell; produces ATP)
  4. In the skeletal system, which cell type is responsible for maintaining bone matrix? (Answer: Osteocyte)

Section 7: SUMMARY 1

The study guide covers the essential bridge between cellular biology and systemic physiology.
