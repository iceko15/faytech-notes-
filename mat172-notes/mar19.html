Polar Graphing & Complex Numbers

BIG IDEA

Transitioning from pure polar coordinate graphing (Section 6.4) into the representation of Complex Numbers in Polar Form (Section 6.5). The core connection is that the $a$ and $b$ components of a complex number $a + bi$ act exactly like $x$ and $y$ rectangular coordinates, allowing us to use the Pythagorean theorem and trigonometry to find the magnitude (modulus) and direction (argument) of the number.

"The thing that burns my hide the most is when y'all just draw some random swirly twirl without a plan. At least map out the landmarks you know for sure—the pole, the intercepts, and the maximum distance. Do me a solid and try to map out what you know!"

KEY TERMS

  • Rectangular Form: The standard $a + bi$ format for complex numbers.
  • Polar Form (Trigonometric Form): Represented as $r(\cos \theta + i \sin \theta)$, often abbreviated as $r \text{ cis } \theta$.
  • Modulus: The absolute value of a complex number, $|z|$, representing the distance from the origin (hypotenuse).
  • Argument: The angle $\theta$ associated with the complex number, usually restricted to $0 \le \theta < 2\pi$.
  • Real Axis: The horizontal axis (replaces the x-axis).
  • Imaginary Axis: The vertical axis (replaces the y-axis).

Section: Complex Numbers in the Polar World

The point $(a, b)$ is plotted as $a + bi$.

  • Modulus ($|z|$): Defined as the hypotenuse: $|z| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$.
  • Argument: Defined as the angle $\theta$.

STEPS/PROCESS

Calculator Setup for Polar Graphing

  1. Mode: Switch to POLAR and RADIAN.
  2. Zooming: Start with Zoom Standard (Option 6) to orient yourself ($[-10, 10]$ window). Use Zoom Square (Option 5) to fix the rectangular distortion so circles don't look like ovals.
  3. Tracing: When zooming in or out, you must hit Enter an extra time to center the zoom on the blinking cursor.
  4. The Theta Gap: If a graph (like a lemniscate $r^2 = a \cos 2\theta$) looks incomplete, go to Window and increase $\theta\text{max}$ from $2\pi$ to $4\pi$. Avoid jumping to a "million $\pi$" or you will burn your battery out waiting for the calculator to finish calculating.
Calculator Orientation: The Reset Strategy: If you lose track of where you are in space, always hit ZOOM STANDARD first to orient yourself. Use ZOOM SQUARE to ensure your circles don't look like ovals.
Pro-Tip: Avoiding the Loading Screen of Death: Do not set your $\theta_{max}$ too high (like $1000\pi$) when trying to view a full polar graph. It is much better to increase $\theta_{max}$ in small increments of $2\pi$ to save battery and processing time.
Technical Alert: TI-Nspire 'Grammar' for Powers: For $\sin^2(\theta)$, you MUST type $(\sin(\theta))^2$ in the calculator. If you don't use parentheses correctly, the calculator squares the angle, not the result!

Converting $a + bi$ to Polar Form

  1. Plot the point $(a, b)$ to determine the quadrant.
  2. Calculate $r$ (the modulus) using $r = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$.
  3. Calculate $\theta$ (the argument) using $\tan \theta = \frac{b}{a}$. Adjust for the quadrant.
  4. Plug into the template: $r(\cos \theta + i \sin \theta)$.
Technical Tip: Centering Your View: When using ZOOM IN or ZOOM OUT, you must hit ENTER a second time to center the screen on your blinking cursor.

The 'Delta Tbl' trick: Set it to $\pi/12$ for professional-grade plotting.

RULES/IDENTITIES

Complex Number Relations

  • $i = \sqrt{-1}$ and $i^2 = -1$.
  • $a = r \cos \theta$
  • $b = r \sin \theta$
  • Modulus: $|z| = r = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$
  • Argument: $\tan \theta = \frac{b}{a}$

EXAMPLE 1: Graphing a Lemniscate

Problem: Graph $r^2 = 9 \cos(2\theta)$.
Steps:
  1. Isolate $r$ by rooting both sides: $r = \pm \sqrt{9 \cos(2\theta)}$.
  2. In the calculator ($Y=$ screen), enter:
    • $r1 = \sqrt{9 \cos(2\theta)}$
    • $r2 = -\sqrt{9 \cos(2\theta)}$
  3. Note: Zoom Standard might only show half the "propeller." Extend $\theta\text{max}$ to $4\pi$ to see the full figure-8 shape.

EXAMPLE 2: Finding the Modulus (Absolute Value)

Problem: Determine the absolute value of $z = -3 + 4i$.
Process:
  1. Recognize this as a 3-4-5 right triangle.
  2. $|z| = \sqrt{(-3)^2 + 4^2} = \sqrt{9 + 16} = \sqrt{25}$.
  3. $|z| = 5$.

EXAMPLE 3: Rectangular to Polar Conversion

Problem: Convert $2 + 2i$ to polar form.
Process:
  1. $r = \sqrt{2^2 + 2^2} = \sqrt{8} = 2\sqrt{2}$.
  2. $\tan \theta = \frac{2}{2} = 1$. Since we are in Quadrant I, $\theta = \frac{\pi}{4}$.
  3. Result: $2\sqrt{2}(\cos \frac{\pi}{4} + i \sin \frac{\pi}{4})$.
"Students often think this doesn't feel like an answer. If you simplify the $\cos$ and $\sin$, you just end up back at $2 + 2i$. The trig expression IS the answer."

PRACTICE PROBLEMS

Problem: Convert $4(\cos 240^\circ + i \sin 240^\circ)$ to rectangular form.
Solution:
  1. Identify Quadrant: $240^\circ$ is in Quadrant III (both $a$ and $b$ must be negative).
  2. Reference Angle: $240^\circ - 180^\circ = 60^\circ$.
  3. Values: $\cos 60^\circ = 1/2$, $\sin 60^\circ = \sqrt{3}/2$.
  4. Substitute: $4(-1/2 + i(-\sqrt{3}/2))$.
  5. Distribute: $-2 - 2\sqrt{3}i$.

Section: The Polar Advantage (Product & Quotient Rules)

Multiplication and division are significantly easier in polar form.

  • Product: $r_1 r_2 [\cos(\theta_1 + \theta_2) + i \sin(\theta_1 + \theta_2)]$
  • Quotient: $\frac{r_1}{r_2} [\cos(\theta_1 - \theta_2) + i \sin(\theta_1 - \theta_2)]$

Note: No FOILing or conjugates required!

Section: DeMoivre’s Theorem (Complex Powers)

Raising complex numbers to powers is the main reason we use polar form. The arithmetic is significantly more efficient than expanding binomials multiple times.

$$z^n = r^n[\cos(n\theta) + i\sin(n\theta)]$$

Step-by-Step Example: To find $(1 + i)^8$

  • Convert to polar: $r = \sqrt{2}$, $\theta = \pi/4$.
  • Apply Theorem: $(\sqrt{2})^8 [\cos(8 \cdot \pi/4) + i \sin(8 \cdot \pi/4)] = 16 [\cos(2\pi) + i \sin(2\pi)] = 16$.

SUMMARY

Whether dealing with Real Polar points ($r, \theta$), Complex Numbers ($a + bi$), or Vectors (coming soon), we are essentially solving the same right triangles. The math is identical; only the labels change. For complex numbers, use the horizontal "Real" axis and the vertical "Imaginary" axis. Use your calculator's complex mode (found in MODE) to check results, but remember it will give you decimals, not exact radicals or $\pi$ values. The power of the polar system isn't just in the plotting, but in the efficiency of the arithmetic for powers and roots.
