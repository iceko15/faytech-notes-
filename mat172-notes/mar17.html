Polar Coordinates & Equation Conversions

BIG IDEA

Transitioning from the rectangular paradigm (horizontal and vertical components) to the polar paradigm (distance from center and angle of rotation). This involves not just plotting points, but renaming them using co-terminal angles and negated radii, and converting entire equations between $x, y$ and $r, \theta$.

"You’re learning a different language. You've used x and y since late elementary or middle school; now you have to change your whole world's worth of thinking about point names. Don't overcomplicate it—common sense and visualization are your best friends."

KEY TERMS

  • Rectangular Coordinates: $(x, y)$ format based on horizontal and vertical displacement.
  • Polar Coordinates: $(r, \theta)$ format where $r$ is the distance from the pole (origin) and $\theta$ is the angle of rotation.
  • Pole: The origin $(0,0)$ in the polar coordinate system.
  • Polar Axis: The positive x-axis from which angles are measured.
  • Quadrantal Angle: An angle whose terminal side lies on a coordinate axis (e.g., $0, \pi/2, \pi, 3\pi/2$).

STEPS/PROCESS

1. Converting Polar to Rectangular $(r, \theta) \to (x, y)$

This relies on basic Right Triangle Trigonometry (SOH-CAH-TOA):

  • $x = r \cos(\theta)$
  • $y = r \sin(\theta)$

2. Converting Rectangular to Polar $(x, y) \to (r, \theta)$

  • Find $r$ using the Pythagorean Theorem: $r^2 = x^2 + y^2 \implies r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$.
  • Find $\theta$ using $\tan(\theta) = \frac{y}{x}$.
    Warning: Inverse tangent only gives values in Quadrants I and IV. You must check your $(x, y)$ coordinates to determine the correct quadrant for $\theta$.

3. Calculator Setup for Polar Graphing

  • Press MODE.
  • Change FUNCTION to POLAR.
  • Ensure you are in RADIAN mode for most graphing.
  • The $X, T, \theta, n$ button will now input $\theta$.
  • Use ZOOM SQUARE to avoid the "stretched" look of the standard screen.

Quadrant Survival Guide for $\theta$

When using $\tan^{-1}(y/x)$ to find your angle, remember that the inverse tangent function is limited to Quadrants I and IV. You must manually check the signs of your rectangular coordinates to apply the correct adjustment.

Quadrant Signs $\theta$ Formula
Q1 $(+x, +y)$ $\theta = \tan^{-1}(y/x)$
Q2 $(-x, +y)$ $\theta = \tan^{-1}(y/x) + \pi$
Q3 $(-x, -y)$ $\theta = \tan^{-1}(y/x) + \pi$
Q4 $(+x, -y)$ $\theta = \tan^{-1}(y/x)$
Shortcut for Unit Circle Reference Angles:
1. If $|\text{opposite/adjacent}| = 1$, reference angle is $45^\circ$ or $\pi/4$.
2. If cosine is $1/2$, reference angle is $60^\circ$ or $\pi/3$.
3. If sine is $1/2$, reference angle is $30^\circ$ or $\pi/6$.

EXAMPLES

Example 1: Polar to Rectangular

Given: $(2, \pi/3)$ in $(r, \theta)$ format.
Step 1: $x = 2 \cos(\pi/3) = 2(1/2) = 1$
Step 2: $y = 2 \sin(\pi/3) = 2(\sqrt{3}/2) = \sqrt{3}$
Final Result: $(1, \sqrt{3})$

Example 2: Rectangular to Polar

Given: $(-2, 2)$ in $(x, y)$ format.
Step 1 (Find r): $r^2 = (-2)^2 + (2)^2 = 4 + 4 = 8 \implies r = \sqrt{8} = 2\sqrt{2}$
Step 2 (Find θ): $\tan(\theta) = \frac{2}{-2} = -1$.
Step 3 (Determine Quadrant): $(-2, 2)$ is in Quadrant II. The reference angle for a tangent of 1 is $\pi/4$. In Quadrant II, $\theta = \pi - \pi/4 = 3\pi/4$.
Final Result: $(2\sqrt{2}, 3\pi/4)$

Example 3: Equation Conversion (Linear)

Convert: $3x + y = 7$ to polar.
Step 1: Replace $x$ and $y$: $3(r \cos\theta) + (r \sin\theta) = 7$
Step 2: Factor out $r$: $r(3 \cos\theta + \sin\theta) = 7$
Step 3: Isolate $r$: $r = \frac{7}{3 \cos\theta + \sin\theta}$

Example 4: Equation Conversion (Circular)

Convert: $r = 10 \sin\theta$ to rectangular.
Prof Tip: Don't square both sides initially (it makes it messy). Instead, multiply both sides by $r$.
Step 1: $r \cdot r = r \cdot 10 \sin\theta \implies r^2 = 10r \sin\theta$
Step 2: Substitute: $x^2 + y^2 = 10y$
Step 3: $x^2 + y^2 - 10y = 0$. By completing the square for $y$ (adding 25 to both sides): $x^2 + (y-5)^2 = 25$.
Observation: This is a circle centered at $(0, 5)$ with a radius of $5$.

SYMMETRY TESTING

While looking at the graph is the easiest way to identify symmetry, formal tests include:

  • Polar Axis (x-axis): Replace $\theta$ with $-\theta$. If equation is unchanged, it's symmetric.
  • Line $\theta = \pi/2$ (y-axis): Replace $\theta$ with $(\pi - \theta)$ or use double negatives.
  • Pole (Origin): Replace $r$ with $-r$.
"The problem with symmetry tests is they don't always work. If it passes, the symmetry is confirmed. If it fails algebraically, the symmetry might still exist! It’s often a complete waste of time unless MyMathLab explicitly asks for it."
Logic Check: The Rules of Verification: Identity verification is NOT solving an equation. You must pick ONE side (usually the more complex one) and manipulate it until it matches the other side. Treat the equal sign as a wall. Do not move terms across it!

Strategy: The Common Denominator "Squish"

When calculating numerical identity answers, separated fractions should be squished into one using an LCD. This is almost always the required format for exam answers.

$$\frac{1}{2} + \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \to \frac{1 + \sqrt{3}}{2}$$

Verification Strategy: The Hierarchy of Moves

If you are stuck on an identity verification problem, try running through these four main moves in order of complexity:

  1. Term Replacement: Convert everything to $\sin$ and $\cos$.
  2. Factoring: Look for a Greatest Common Factor (GCF) or a Difference of Squares.
  3. LCD Consolidation: Add separated fractions together using a common denominator.
  4. Conjugate Multiplication: Multiply by the conjugate to force a Pythagorean Identity.

Conjugate Example:

$$\frac{1}{1+\sin\theta} \cdot \frac{1-\sin\theta}{1-\sin\theta} = \frac{1-\sin\theta}{\cos^2\theta}$$

Visual Shortcut: The Pieces of Pie Method

When finding renamed polar points, counting slices (e.g., quarters = $\pi/4$) around the unit circle is often safer than relying entirely on arithmetic.

Example: To find the negative coterminal angle for $3\pi/4$, count 5 "quarter" slices clockwise around the circle to land at $-5\pi/4$.

SUMMARY

Always start by visualizing the location of a point or the general shape of an equation. Use the calculator to verify your conversions, but remember that the exam requires exact values (roots and fractions) rather than decimal approximations. For circle conversions, multiplying by $r$ is often more efficient than squaring both sides.
