BIG IDEA

We are wrapping up Chapter 4 by reviewing graphing mechanics, defining the strict domain/range restrictions for Inverse Trig Functions (4.7), and applying trigonometry to real-world scenarios like Bearings and Harmonic Motion (4.8).

SECTION 4.5/4.6: GRAPHING REVIEW

When graphing $y = A \sin(Bx - C) + D$, calculation errors usually happen in the Phase Shift or the Calculator Window settings.

1. The Phase Shift Trap

Many students look at $Bx - C$ and assume the shift is just $C$. This is incorrect if $B \neq 1$.

Correct Method:

Set the argument equal to zero and solve for $x$: $$Bx - C = 0 \Rightarrow x = \frac{C}{B}$$

2. Calculator Strategy (Window Settings)

The default calculator window ($-10$ to $10$) is useless for trig. You must manually configure the Window to match your specific function.

  • X-Min / X-Max: Set this to cover at least one full period (e.g., $-\pi$ to $3\pi$).
  • X-Scale: This is the most important setting. Set it to your Quarter Point increment ($\text{Period} / 4$). This forces the tick marks to align with your max, min, and intercepts.
  • Table Set (2nd + Window): Match your $\Delta Tbl$ to your X-Scale so the table values aren't random decimals.

SECTION 4.7: INVERSE TRIG FUNCTIONS

Inverse functions ($f^{-1}$) swap inputs and outputs. For trig, the input is a ratio (side lengths), and the output is an angle.

The "Monkey Wrench": To make inverses functions, we must restrict their ranges. The calculator will only give answers in specific quadrants.

Function Notation Restricted Range (Output Quadrants)
Inverse Sine $\sin^{-1}(x)$ or $\arcsin(x)$ Q1 & Q4 $[-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}]$
Inverse Tangent $\tan^{-1}(x)$ or $\arctan(x)$ Q1 & Q4 $(-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2})$
Inverse Cosine $\cos^{-1}(x)$ or $\arccos(x)$ Q1 & Q2 $[0, \pi]$
Critical Warning: If you are solving for $\sin^{-1}$ and the ratio is negative, the calculator gives a negative angle (Q4). Do NOT use the Q3 angle (like $7\pi/4$) even if it has the same sine value, because it is outside the restricted range.

SECTION 4.8: APPLICATIONS & MODELS

1. Solving Right Triangles

Standard SOH-CAH-TOA problems. If looking for a side, use algebra. If looking for an angle, use the inverse function.

Angle of Elevation/Depression: Always measured from the Horizontal. Elevation is looking up; Depression is looking down.

2. Navigational Bearings

In math (unlike some boy scout methods), bearings are given in a 3-part format: N/S [Angle] E/W.

  • Start: North or South line (y-axis).
  • Turn: The specified number of degrees towards East or West.
  • Example: $S 31^\circ W$ means start pointing South, then turn $31^\circ$ towards the West.
Example: A ship travels 2.3 miles at $S 31^\circ W$, then turns $90^\circ$ right and travels 3.5 miles. Find the bearing from the start.
Strategy: Draw the diagram. The turn creates a right triangle. Use $\tan(\theta) = \frac{\text{opp}}{\text{adj}}$ to find the angle inside the triangle, then add/subtract from the original bearing to find the final direction.

3. Simple Harmonic Motion

Used for springs, pendulums, or anything bouncing up and down. $$d = a \cos(\omega t) \quad \text{or} \quad d = a \sin(\omega t)$$

  • $d$: Displacement from rest.
  • $a$: Maximum Displacement (Amplitude).
  • $\omega$ (Omega): Angular frequency (replaces $B$).
  • Period: Time for one cycle $= \frac{2\pi}{\omega}$.
  • Frequency: Cycles per unit of time $= \frac{\omega}{2\pi}$ (Reciprocal of Period).
Example: An object moves with motion $d = 12 \cos(\frac{\pi}{4}t)$.
  • Max Displacement: $12$ (Amplitude).
  • Period: $\frac{2\pi}{\pi/4} = 2\pi \cdot \frac{4}{\pi} = 8$ seconds.
  • Frequency: $\frac{1}{8}$ oscillation per second.

SUMMARY

For the test, ensure you can manually graph trig functions by finding the period and quarter-points. Memorize the range restrictions for inverse functions (especially that Sine/Tangent use negative Q4 angles, not positive 300-degree angles). For word problems, draw the picture—bearings start from the vertical axis, and elevation starts from the horizontal.
