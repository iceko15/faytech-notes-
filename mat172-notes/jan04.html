Unit Circle, Identities, and Oscillations

BIG IDEA

The mastery of trigonometry involves understanding the Unit Circle as a coordinate system, the periodic nature of trigonometric functions as waves, and the algebraic manipulation of identities to simplify complex periodic reactions.

KEY TERMS

Term Definition
Unit Circle A circle with a radius of \( r = 1 \), centered at the origin, used to define trigonometric ratios for all angles.
Phase Shift A horizontal translation of a periodic function, represented by the variable \( C \).
Periodicity The quality of a function repeating its values in regular intervals or cycles (e.g., \( 360^\circ \) or \( 2\pi \) radians).
Oblique Triangle Any triangle that does not contain a right angle; solved using Law of Sines or Law of Cosines.
Asymptote A line that a graph approaches but never touches, frequently found in tangent and reciprocal functions.

STEPS/PROCESS

  1. Convert Degrees to Radians: Use the direct conversion factor where \( 180^\circ = \pi \) radians.
  2. Apply SOHCAHTOA: Utilize the reference triangle model to determine sine, cosine, and tangent based on the opposite, adjacent, and hypotenuse sides.
  3. Identify Transformations: Apply horizontal transformations (phase shifts) first, followed by vertical translations.
  4. Determine Periodicity: Calculate the frequency and period of harmonic motion to understand how waves oscillate over time.

RULES & IDENTITIES

  • Pythagorean Identity: \[ \sin^2(\theta) + \cos^2(\theta) = 1 \]
  • Reciprocal System: Sine corresponds with Cosecant, and Cosine corresponds with Secant.
  • Normal Range: A full circle set point is \( 360^\circ \). Variations up to \( \pm 10^\circ \) (e.g., \( 350^\circ \) to \( 370^\circ \)) are often considered within a "normal" range before systemic adjustments are needed.
  • Phase Reactions:
    • Negative Phase: Acts to bring a system back to its normal periodic range (homeostasis).
    • Positive Phase: Amplifies a reaction, making it greater and moving it further from the set point.

EXAMPLE/DEMONSTRATION

1. Coordinate Extraction

On the unit circle, the coordinates are defined as \( (x, y) = (\cos \theta, \sin \theta) \). For an angle of \( 60^\circ \), the coordinate is \( (\frac{1}{2}, \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}) \).

2. Law of Sines Application

Used for oblique triangles when given AAS or ASA. The ratio is defined as: \[ \frac{a}{\sin A} = \frac{b}{\sin B} = \frac{c}{\sin C} \]

3. pH Scale Relationship

While often used in chemistry, the logarithmic nature of pH follows mathematical periodic modeling. High \( H^+ \) concentration = Low pH (Acidic); High \( OH^- \) concentration = High pH (Basic).

PRACTICE PROBLEMS

  • If \( \sin(\theta) = \frac{1}{2} \) and the terminal side is in Quadrant II, what is \( \cos(\theta) \)? (Answer: \( -\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \))
  • Identify the transformation order for \( y = \sin(x - C) + D \). (Answer: Horizontal phase shift \( C \) first, then vertical translation \( D \))
  • What is the reciprocal of the Sine function? (Answer: Cosecant / \( \csc \))

SUMMARY

Trigonometry is the study of how angles, circles, and waves interact. By using the unit circle as a set point, we can model everything from simple triangles to complex harmonic motion and pH oscillations. Mastery of identities like the Pythagorean theorem and reciprocal ratios is essential for solving these periodic systems.
