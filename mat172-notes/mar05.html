Solving Oblique Triangles: Law of Sines & Cosines

BIG IDEA

Solving a triangle means calculating all missing sides and angles based on a set of given parts. Unlike right-angle trigonometry, oblique triangles require the Law of Sines or the Law of Cosines. The choice of formula depends entirely on the combination of parts provided (SAS, AAS, SSS, etc.). Special attention must be paid to the Ambiguous Case (SSA), where a single set of measurements may result in zero, one, or two distinct triangles.

Always sketch a "rough doodle" of the triangle. Visualizing the proportions—like which side is longest—helps predict if an angle should be obtuse. If you find yourself solving for an obtuse angle, be wary of the Law of Sines; the Law of Cosines is safer for obtuse calculations.

KEY TERMS

  • Oblique Triangle: A triangle that does not contain a right angle ($90^\circ$).
  • Law of Sines: A ratio-based formula used when you have a matching "pair" of an angle and its opposite side.
  • Law of Cosines: A formula relating three sides and one angle; necessary for SSS or SAS scenarios.
  • Ambiguous Case (SSA): A scenario where two sides and a non-included angle are given. It is nicknamed the "donkey scenario" (side-side-angle) because it causes the most trouble.
  • Dangly Side: The side opposite the given angle in an SSA scenario. Its length relative to the "height" ($h$) determines the number of solutions.
  • Isosceles Triangle: A triangle with two equal sides and two equal angles.

STEPS/PROCESS

1. Identification of Triangle Type

Scenario Acronym Method to Solve
Two angles and a non-included side AAS Law of Sines
Two angles and the included side ASA Law of Sines
Two sides and the included angle SAS Law of Cosines
Three sides SSS Law of Cosines
Two sides and a non-included angle SSA Ambiguous Case (Check height $h$)

2. Solving the Ambiguous Case (SSA)

To determine the number of solutions when given Angle $A$, Side $a$ (dangly side), and Side $b$ (adjacent side):

  1. Calculate the vertical height: $h = b \sin(A)$.
  2. Zero Solutions: If $a < h$, the side is too short to reach the base.
  3. One Solution: If $a \geq b$ or $a = h$.
  4. Two Solutions: If $h < a < b$, the dangly side can swing inward or outward to form two different triangles.
If you hate memorizing the height conditions, just try solving using the Law of Sines. If the calculator gives you an "ERROR" during the inverse sine step, there is no solution.

RULES/IDENTITIES

  • Angle Sum: $A + B + C = 180^\circ$ (Always check this first if two angles are given).
  • Law of Sines: $$\frac{a}{\sin A} = \frac{b}{\sin B} = \frac{c}{\sin C}$$ (Use two parts at a time).
  • Law of Cosines: $$a^2 = b^2 + c^2 - 2bc \cos A$$ (And its variations for $b^2$ and $c^2$).
  • Obtuse Rule: An obtuse angle, if it exists, will always be across from the longest side.

EXAMPLE 1: AAS (The "Easy" Way)

Given: $A = 48^\circ$, $B = 37^\circ$, $a = 100$. Find missing parts.

Step 1: Find Angle C.
$C = 180^\circ - 48^\circ - 37^\circ = 95^\circ$.

Step 2: Use Law of Sines for Side $b$.
$\frac{100}{\sin 48^\circ} = \frac{b}{\sin 37^\circ} \implies b = \frac{100 \sin 37^\circ}{\sin 48^\circ} \approx 80.978$ units.

Step 3: Use Law of Sines for Side $c$.
$\frac{100}{\sin 48^\circ} = \frac{c}{\sin 95^\circ} \implies c = \frac{100 \sin 95^\circ}{\sin 48^\circ} \approx 134.013$ units.

EXAMPLE 2: SSA - No Solution

Given: $A = 30^\circ$, $a = 10$, $b = 40$.

Calculation:
Let's find the height $h$: $h = 40 \sin 30^\circ = 40(0.5) = 20$.
Since the opposite side ($a = 10$) is shorter than the height ($h = 20$), the side cannot reach the horizontal base.
Result: No Triangle/No Solution.

EXAMPLE 3: SSA - One Solution

Given: $A = 40^\circ$, $a = 20$, $b = 15$.

Analysis: Since $a > b$ (20 > 15), the dangly side is longer than the adjacent side. It can only swing "outward" to form one triangle.

$\frac{20}{\sin 40^\circ} = \frac{15}{\sin B} \implies \sin B = \frac{15 \sin 40^\circ}{20} \implies B = \sin^{-1}(0.482) \approx 28.82^\circ$.
$C = 180^\circ - 40^\circ - 28.82^\circ = 111.18^\circ$.
Side $c = \frac{20 \sin 111.18^\circ}{\sin 40^\circ} \approx 29.04$.

SUMMARY

  • Calculator Mode: Ensure your calculator is in DEGREE mode. Wrong modes often give answers that look "close" but are subtly wrong.
  • Parentheses: Always close parentheses after the angle in sine/cosine functions: $\sin(40)/20$, not $\sin(40/20)$.
  • Rounding: Do not round intermediate steps. Keep as many decimals as possible in your calculator until the very final answer for MyMathLab.
  • Notation: Label angles with capital letters and degree symbols ($^\circ$) and sides with lowercase letters to avoid confusing the two.
