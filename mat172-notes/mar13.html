Polar Coordinates and Exam Review

BIG IDEA

Polar coordinates provide a way to describe points in a plane using a distance from the pole ($r$) and an angle from the polar axis ($\theta$). Unlike Cartesian coordinates, a single point in space can have multiple "names" or representations in polar form by manipulating the sign of $r$ or using coterminal angles.

"One of the things that irritates me about this book sometimes is that they don't make you doodle enough pictures. This is a very visual subject. Showing what the question is asking visually helps just as much as knowing how to do the arithmetic and memorizing a random calculation."

KEY TERMS

  • Co-terminal Name: Different coordinate pairs that represent the exact same physical space or point.
  • R-Value ($r$): The directed distance from the origin. If $r$ is negative, you move in the opposite direction of the specified angle.
  • Concentric Rings: The circles on a polar grid that represent increasing distance from the center. In today's examples, rings are counted by increments of 2.

STEPS/PROCESS

Renaming a Point with a Negative $r$ Value

  1. Identify the target point on the polar grid.
  2. Look "directly across" from that point (180 degrees or $\pi$ radians away).
  3. Identify the angle in that opposite quadrant.
  4. Use the negative version of the $r$ value with this new angle. Because the $r$ is negative, you will travel "away" from the new angle and land back on your original point.

Renaming a Point with a Negative Angle Requirement

  1. Locate the original angle (e.g., $3\pi/4$).
  2. To find the negative name within one rotation, subtract $2\pi$ from the original angle.
  3. Alternatively, count "quarters" or "sixths" around the circle in the clockwise direction to find the visual name.

RULES/IDENTITIES

  • The "Directly Across" Rule: To negate $r$ and stay at the same point, you must change the angle by exactly $\pi$ (or 180°). Formula: $(r, \theta) = (-r, \theta + \pi)$.
  • Coterminal Angle Calculation: $\theta_{new} = \theta_{original} \pm 2\pi n$.
  • Sign of $r$:
    • Positive $r$: Move toward the angle.
    • Negative $r$: Move away from the angle (through the pole).

The Negative r Challenge: The 'Straight-Edge' Rule

When $r$ is negative, you must look directly across the pole ($180^\circ$ or $\pi$ radians away) to find the correct angle to aim toward before walking "backwards."

Step-by-Step Example: Renaming $(10, 3\pi/4)$ with a negative $r$:

  1. Locate Original Angle: Find $3\pi/4$ on the unit circle (Quadrant II).
  2. Apply Straight-Edge Rule: Look directly across the pole to Quadrant IV. Add $\pi$ (or $4\pi/4$) to get $7\pi/4$.
  3. Negate $r$: Use the new angle but make the radius negative. Traveling $-10$ units from $7\pi/4$ lands you back at the original spot.
  4. Result: $(-10, 7\pi/4)$

EXAMPLE 3: Renaming with Negative Angle

Original Point: $(10, 3\pi/4)$

Goal: Keep $r = 10$ (positive), but use a negative angle $\theta < 0$.

  • Start at the polar axis and move clockwise to reach the point at $3\pi/4$.
  • Counting by quarters: $1, 2, 3, 4, 5$ quarters in the negative direction.
  • Visual Result: $-5\pi/4$.
  • Arithmetic Result: $\frac{3\pi}{4} - 2\pi = \frac{3\pi}{4} - \frac{8\pi}{4} = -\frac{5\pi}{4}$.

The Conversion Bridge: From Polar to Cartesian

To convert coordinates or equations between the two systems, we rely on four core formulas derived from right-triangle trigonometry:

  • $x = r \cos(\theta)$
  • $y = r \sin(\theta)$
  • $r^2 = x^2 + y^2$
  • $\tan(\theta) = \frac{y}{x}$
[ Placeholder: Insert Conversion Bridge Diagram Here ]
Quadrant Caution: The $\theta$ Correction: When using $\tan^{-1}(y/x)$ to find $\theta$, you MUST check your quadrant. Because the inverse tangent function only returns values in Quadrants I and IV, if your Cartesian point $(x, y)$ is in Quadrant II or III, you must add $\pi$ (or $180^\circ$) to your calculated $\theta$.
Pro-Tip: Equation Conversion Hacks: When converting polar equations to Cartesian form, the "Multiply by $r$" trick is often much easier than attempting to substitute complex fractions or square both sides right away.

Example: Convert $r = 4\sin\theta$ to Cartesian form.
  • Step 1 (Multiply by $r$): $r^2 = 4r \sin\theta$
  • Step 2 (Substitute): $x^2 + y^2 = 4y$

Symmetry Cheat Sheet

When graphing polar equations, use these substitution tests to determine symmetry. If the equation remains unchanged after substitution, the symmetry is confirmed.

Symmetry Type Substitution Test
Polar Axis (x-axis) Replace $\theta$ with $-\theta$
The Pole (Origin) Replace $r$ with $-r$
Line $\theta = \pi/2$ (y-axis) Replace $\theta$ with $\pi - \theta$

SUMMARY

Mastering polar coordinates requires comfort with fractions and the ability to visualize the unit circle. A point is simply a location; $r$ and $\theta$ are directions to get there. If you change the sign of $r$, you must compensate by shifting the angle by 180 degrees. In exam performance, focus on recognizing formula patterns and always grounding your work in sketches of triangles or circles.

Classroom Motto:
"A straight-edge and a sketch are better than a formula and a prayer."
