Trigonometry: Units, Identities, and Harmonic Motion

BIG IDEA

This review connects the foundational mechanics of the unit circle and trigonometric identities to real-world oscillations, harmonic motion, and periodic biological systems.

KEY TERMS

Term Definition/Context
Unit Circle A circle with a radius of 1, where coordinates correspond to (cos θ, sin θ).
Phase Shift A horizontal translation of a periodic function (C before D logic).
Periodicity The quality of repeating at regular intervals (set point of 360° for a full circle).
Harmonic Motion Oscillatory motion where period can be broken down into frequency.

STEPS: Transformations & Conversions

  1. Horizontal First: Address horizontal translations (Phase Shifts) before moving to vertical translations.
  2. Direct Pi Conversion: Utilize the relationship between degrees and radians to translate angle measures.
  3. Amplitude Assessment: Determine the layer of oscillation (height of the wave) in the sine or tangent layers.
  4. Identify Inverses: Use Arccosine and Arcsine to link the ratio system back to the angle system.

RULES: Oscillations & Phases

  • Sine Layer: Where most oscillation and high-periodic activity occurs.
  • Tangent Layer: Characterized by the presence of asymptotes.
  • Positive Phase: Makes a reaction or wave greater/larger.
  • Negative Phase: Acts to bring the system back to periodicity (homeostasis).
  • Normal Range: For a 360° set point, the acceptable "wiggle room" is typically between 359.5° and 361°.

EXAMPLES

1. Pythagorean Identity:
sin2θ + cos2θ = 1. These identities act as "exact copies" or duplicates of the original function values.
2. Oblique Triangles:
Use Law of Sines and Law of Cosines. Remember types like SSS (Side-Side-Side) and SAS (Side-Angle-Side) for non-right triangles.
3. pH as a Logarithmic Scale:
Low pH (0-2) indicates high H+ concentration (acidic), while high pH indicates high OH- concentration (basic).

PRACTICE

  • If the set point is 360°, at what value does the instructor start to "worry" about the deviation? (Answer: Over 370°).
  • Which function forms the range when using the central value system? (Answer: Inverse functions).
  • What is the role of angular velocity in the VTC? (Answer: Helps produce total speed/velocity).

SUMMARY

The course focuses on the synthesis of the unit circle, periodic functions, and their application to harmonic motion. Key emphasis is placed on understanding the "normal range" of oscillation and the mathematical identities (reciprocal and Pythagorean) that allow us to manipulate complex waves.
