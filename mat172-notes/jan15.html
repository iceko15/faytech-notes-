Unit Circle & Radian Conversions

Date: January 15, 2026

BIG IDEA

Transitioning from degree-based geometry to radian-based trigonometry by understanding the Unit Circle as a set of patterns and mastering the dimensional analysis required to convert between units of angular measure.

KEY TERMS

Term Definition
Radian The measure of a central angle that intercepts an arc equal in length to the radius of the circle. \(1 \text{ radian} \approx 57.3^\circ\).
Arc Length (\(s\)) The distance traveled along the edge of a circle, calculated as \(s = r\theta\).
Co-terminal Angles Two angles that share the same terminal (ending) side but differ by full rotations (\(2\pi\) or \(360^\circ\)).
Hypotenuse The side specifically opposite the right angle in a right triangle.

STEPS: CONVERTING UNITS

  1. Identify the starting unit: Determine if you are starting with degrees or radians.
  2. Apply the Conversion Factor:
    • To Degrees: Multiply by \(\frac{180^\circ}{\pi}\).
    • To Radians: Multiply by \(\frac{\pi}{180^\circ}\).
  3. Simplify the Fraction: Reduce numbers and cancel units (dimensional analysis). Keep \(\pi\) in the final answer for "Exact Value."

RULES FOR RADIAN MEASURE

  • A full rotation (\(360^\circ\)) is exactly \(2\pi\) radians.
  • A half rotation (\(180^\circ\)) is exactly \(\pi\) radians.
  • If no unit is written for an angle, it is assumed to be in radians.
  • Exact Value means no decimals; leave the answer as a simplified fraction containing \(\pi\).

EXAMPLE/DEMONSTRATION

1. Finding Theta from Arc Length

Scenario: Radius (\(r\)) = 12 ft, Arc Length (\(s\)) = 42 ft. Find \(\theta\).

\[ \theta = \frac{s}{r} = \frac{42 \text{ ft}}{12 \text{ ft}} \]

The units (feet) cancel out, leaving: \[ \theta = 3.5 \text{ radians} \text{ (or } \frac{7}{2} \text{)} \]

2. Degree to Radian (Exact)

Scenario: Convert \(60^\circ\) to Radians.

\[ 60^\circ \times \frac{\pi}{180^\circ} = \frac{60\pi}{180} \]

Simplify by dividing both by 60: \[ \text{Exact Value: } \frac{\pi}{3} \]

3. Calculator Strategy for Fractions

If the calculator gives a decimal like \(1.047\), pull the \(\pi\) away: \[ \frac{60}{180} = 0.333... \xrightarrow{\text{MATH > FRAC}} \frac{1}{3} \]

Then "tack" the \(\pi\) back on top to get \(\frac{\pi}{3}\).

PRACTICE PROBLEMS

  • Convert \(45^\circ\) to Radians. (\(\frac{\pi}{4}\))
  • Convert \(\frac{3\pi}{2}\) to Degrees. (\(270^\circ\))
  • Find the arc length (\(s\)) if \(r = 10\) and \(\theta = 2\) radians. (\(20\))

SUMMARY

The Unit Circle is built on patterns—specifically segments of \(\pi/4\) and \(\pi/6\). When working with these values, precision is vital; "Exact Values" require fraction form with \(\pi\), whereas decimal approximations are only used when requested for rounding. Always remember that the orientation of a triangle doesn't change the definitions of opposite, adjacent, or hypotenuse; those are determined by the angle of interest (\(\alpha\)).
