Linear Speed, Angular Speed, and Trig Foundations

BIG IDEA

The transition from Section 4.1 into 4.4 involves moving from basic rotational concepts (arc length, radians) into the mechanical application of Trigonometric functions. The core objective is to master the relationship between linear motion and angular rotation, while building a geometric intuition for the six trigonometric functions through the Unit Circle and Right Triangle trigonometry.

"Garbage In, Garbage Out": Always evaluate your numbers. If you calculate an arc length of 270 inches for a 6-inch radius circle at 45 degrees, something is wrong. Stop trusting the calculator blindly and use your "gut check" to see if the value is in the realm of reality.

KEY TERMS

  • Linear Speed ($v$): Distance per unit of time (e.g., miles per hour, inches per minute). Defined as $v = \frac{s}{t}$ where $s$ is arc length.
  • Angular Speed ($\omega$ - Omega): How fast the angle changes, regardless of the circle's size. Defined as $\omega = \frac{\theta}{t}$ where $\theta$ is in radians.
  • Co-terminal Angles: Two angles that stop at the same place on a rotation. Calculated by adding or subtracting full revolutions ($2\pi$ or $360^{\circ}$).
  • Reference Angle: The acute angle formed by the terminal side and the x-axis.

FORMULAS & IDENTITIES

The "Big Three" Reciprocals

  • $\csc(\theta) = \frac{1}{\sin(\theta)} = \frac{\text{hypotenuse}}{\text{opposite}}$
  • $\sec(\theta) = \frac{1}{\cos(\theta)} = \frac{\text{hypotenuse}}{\text{adjacent}}$
  • $\cot(\theta) = \frac{1}{\tan(\theta)} = \frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{opposite}}$

Quotient Identity

$$\tan(\theta) = \frac{\sin(\theta)}{\cos(\theta)}$$

Pythagorean Identity

$$\sin^2(\theta) + \cos^2(\theta) = 1$$

"I don't follow the formula specifically because I think it's more complicated... it uses wonky symbols." Use Dimensional Analysis (Unit Conversion) instead. Make the units cancel out, and the math will follow.

STEPS/PROCESS

Unit Circle Hand Trick (Quadrant I)

  1. Hold out your left hand, palm facing you.
  2. Pinky is $0^{\circ}$ ($0$ rad), Thumb is $90^{\circ}$ ($\pi/2$ rad).
  3. Fingers in between represent $30^{\circ}, 45^{\circ}, 60^{\circ}$.
  4. To find $\sin$ and $\cos$ of $60^{\circ}$ ($\pi/3$): Fold down your index finger.
    • Fingers to the left (above) = 1. Numerator for $x$ ($\cos$) is $\sqrt{1}$.
    • Fingers to the right (below) = 3. Numerator for $y$ ($\sin$) is $\sqrt{3}$.
    • Denominator is always 2. Result: $(\frac{1}{2}, \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2})$.

Calculating Linear Speed via Dimensional Analysis

  1. Start with the angular speed (usually in Revolutions per Minute).
  2. Write it as a vertical fraction: $\frac{45 \text{ rev}}{1 \text{ min}}$.
  3. Convert Revolutions to Distance: One revolution is the circumference ($2\pi r$).
  4. Multiply: $(\frac{\text{rev}}{\text{min}}) \times (\frac{2\pi r \text{ inches}}{1 \text{ rev}})$.
  5. Cancel units and simplify.

EXAMPLE 1: The Vinyl Record

Problem: A record spins at 45 RPM. The needle is 1.5 inches from the center. Find the linear speed in inches per minute.

Setup: $$\frac{45 \text{ rev}}{1 \text{ min}} \times \frac{2\pi(1.5) \text{ inches}}{1 \text{ rev}}$$
Calculation: $45 \times 3\pi = 135\pi \text{ inches/min}$
Decimal Approximation: $\approx 424 \text{ inches/min}$

EXAMPLE 2: Exact Value with Quadrant Check

Problem: Find $\sec(-\frac{9\pi}{4})$.

Step 1: Simplify Angle (Periodic Property). $-\frac{9\pi}{4}$ is $-2\frac{1}{4}\pi$. Adding $2\pi$ (one revolution) gives $-\frac{\pi}{4}$.
Step 2: Locate Quadrant. $-\frac{\pi}{4}$ is in Quadrant IV. In QIV: $x$ (adjacent) is positive, $y$ (opposite) is negative.
Step 3: Identify Coordinates. At $\frac{\pi}{4}$ reference, values are $(\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}, -\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2})$.
Step 4: Reciprocal function. $\sec = \frac{1}{\cos}$. $$\sec(-\frac{\pi}{4}) = \frac{1}{\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}} = \frac{2}{\sqrt{2}}$$ Step 5: Rationalize. $\frac{2\sqrt{2}}{2} = \sqrt{2}$.

PRACTICE PROBLEMS & CALCULATOR TIPS

Calculator Mode Warning: If you are entering $\pi/10$, you MUST be in Radian mode. If you are entering $45^{\circ}$, you MUST be in Degree mode. The calculator will not auto-correct this for you. Check the top of the screen on newer models.
  • To find $\csc(1.5)$: There is no $\csc$ button. Enter $1 / \sin(1.5)$ in Radian mode.
  • Mixed Numbers on TI-84: Use Alpha + Y= to convert improper fractions to mixed numbers (helpful for finding rotations).
  • Standard Radius: Unless specified otherwise, assume the radius ($r$) is 1 (The Unit Circle).

SUMMARY

Success in this content hinge on three things: 1. Knowing Quadrant I of the Unit Circle by heart (use the hand trick). 2. Forgetting your fear of fractions (use the calculator's fraction bars). 3. Drawing the triangle. If the leg is longer than the hypotenuse, the triangle is impossible—don't waste time calculating a lie.
