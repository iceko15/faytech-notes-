BIG IDEA

Compounds are formed when atoms interact to achieve a neutral charge. The naming and formula-writing conventions depend entirely on whether the compound is Ionic (Metal + Non-metal) or Molecular (Non-metal + Non-metal).

KEY TERMS

  • Ionic Compound: A compound formed from the attraction between a cation (positive) and an anion (negative). Electrons are transferred from one atom to another.
  • Crystal Lattice: A three-dimensional structure of alternating positive and negative charges in all directions.
  • Empirical Formula: The lowest whole-number ratio of ions in an ionic compound.
  • Polyatomic Ion: An ion made of multiple atoms bonded together that carry a collective charge.
  • Binary Molecular Compound: Compounds containing only non-metals, which do not rely on charge balance for their formulas.

POLYATOMIC IONS TO MEMORIZE

While there are dozens of polyatomic ions, the following six are the most common in commercial products and the only ones required for the test:

Name Formula Charge
Ammonium $NH_4^+$ +1
Hydroxide $OH^-$ -1
Nitrate $NO_3^-$ -1
Carbonate $CO_3^{2-}$ -2
Sulfate $SO_4^{2-}$ -2
Phosphate $PO_4^{3-}$ -3
Note: Ammonium is the only common polyatomic cation (positive). All others on this list are anions (negative). In formulas like $NH_4^+$, the 4 is the subscript (number of atoms), not the charge.

STEPS: WRITING IONIC FORMULAS

  1. Identify the charge of the cation and anion using the periodic table or memory (for polyatomics).
  2. Use the Criss-Cross Method: Take the numerical value of the cation's charge and make it the subscript for the anion. Take the numerical value of the anion's charge and make it the subscript for the cation.
  3. Simplify: Ensure the ratio is the lowest whole number (Empirical Formula). For example, $Mg_2O_2$ must be simplified to $MgO$.
  4. Parentheses: Use parentheses around polyatomic ions ONLY if there is more than one of them (e.g., $Mg(NO_3)_2$).

RULES: NAMING IONIC COMPOUNDS

  • Cation (Metal): Named first. The name does not change (e.g., Sodium stays Sodium).
  • Anion (Non-metal): Named second. Change the ending to -ide (e.g., Chlorine becomes Chloride).
  • Polyatomic Ions: Do not change their names. Use them exactly as listed (e.g., Sulfate stays Sulfate).
  • Transition Metals: Metals with variable charges require a Roman Numeral in parentheses to indicate the charge (e.g., Iron (II) means $Fe^{2+}$, Iron (III) means $Fe^{3+}$).

RULES: NAMING MOLECULAR COMPOUNDS (Non-metals only)

Since these do not have charges, we use prefixes to indicate the number of atoms:

  • 1: Mono- (Never used for the first element)
  • 2: Di-
  • 3: Tri-
  • 4: Tetra-
  • 5: Penta-
  • 6: Hexa-
  • 7: Hepta-
  • 8: Octa-
  • 9: Nona-
  • 10: Deca-

ACID NAMING (The "Most Complicated" Rules)

Anion Ending Acid Prefix/Suffix Example
-ide hydro- ______ -ic acid Chloride → Hydrochloric acid
-ate (no prefix) ______ -ic acid Nitrate → Nitric acid
-ite (no prefix) ______ -ous acid Nitrite → Nitrous acid
Professor's Hint: These are the three strong acids you will see most often in lab: Hydrochloric Acid, Nitric Acid, and Sulfuric Acid ($H_2SO_4$).

EXAMPLE PROBLEMS

1. Magnesium and Nitrogen

Magnesium (Group 2): $Mg^{2+}$ | Nitrogen (Group 15): $N^{3-}$

Criss-cross: The 2 becomes the N subscript; the 3 becomes the Mg subscript.

Result: $Mg_3N_2$

2. Barium and Nitrate

Barium (Group 2): $Ba^{2+}$ | Nitrate: $NO_3^-$

We need two Nitrates to balance one Barium. Use parentheses.

Result: $Ba(NO_3)_2$

3. Tungsten (VI) and Oxygen

Tungsten: $W^{6+}$ | Oxygen: $O^{2-}$

Criss-cross gives $W_2O_6$. Simplify to lowest ratio (divide by 2).

Result: $WO_3$

PRACTICE PROBLEMS

  • $NH_4Br$: Ammonium Bromide (Ionic)
  • $Ca(NO_3)_2$: Calcium Nitrate (Ionic)
  • $MnSO_4$: Manganese (II) Sulfate (Ionic - Manganese is transition, Sulfate is 2-, so Mn must be 2+)
  • $SF_6$: Sulfur Hexafluoride (Molecular - use prefixes)
  • $C_3O_2$: Tricarbon Dioxide (Molecular)
  • $TiO_2$: Titanium (IV) Oxide (Ionic - Oxygen is 2-, two Oxygens = 4-, so Titanium must be 4+)

SUMMARY

Always identify the bond type first. If a metal is present, balance charges and use Roman numerals if necessary. If only non-metals are present, use Greek prefixes. Polyatomic ions act as single units—never change their internal subscripts or their names.
