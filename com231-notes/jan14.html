Course Layout & Introduction Speech

Instructor: Rachel Rial (Professor Rial)
Date: January 14, 2026

BIG IDEA

Success in COM-231 relies on understanding that public speaking is an art, not a science. The course is designed to build skills incrementally through "extemporaneous" delivery, prioritizing preparation and audience engagement over rote memorization or reading from a script.

KEY TERMS

Term Definition
Extemporaneous A style of speaking that is prepared and practiced in advance, delivered using brief notes rather than a full script or memorization.
Self-Critique An assignment where students record their speech, watch it, and grade themselves using the provided rubric to identify areas for improvement.
Oral Citations Verbally attributing research sources within the speech (e.g., "According to a 2024 study by the Mayo Clinic...").
VitalSource The "middleman" platform used to redeem codes for the McGraw Hill e-book.

RULES & POLICIES

  • Attendance: Missing 20% of the class (6 absences) results in an automatic withdrawal. Always communicate if an emergency arises.
  • Quiz Priority: McGraw Hill quizzes are for practice and do not count toward your grade. Only the quizzes inside Canvas contribute to your 10% quiz grade.
  • No Reading: Reading your speech directly from a paper or screen is strictly prohibited and will negatively impact your grade. Use limited note cards.
  • Assignment Weight: Speeches and their associated documents (outlines/slides) account for the majority of the course grade.

SPEECH BREAKDOWN

  • Introduction (Minor): 2–4 Minutes. An object-based comparison to introduce yourself.
  • Informative (Major): 5–7 Minutes. Requires research and visual aids (PowerPoint/Google Slides).
  • Persuasive (Major): 6–8 Minutes. The highest-weighted assignment; requires research and visual aids.
  • Ceremonial (Minor): 2–4 Minutes. A shorter speech to conclude the semester; no research or slides required.

EXAMPLE: INTRODUCTION SPEECH BRAINSTORMING

The Task: Choose an object that represents you and compare yourself to it in 2–3 ways.

Example 1: A Compass
1. I am always looking for a clear direction in life.
2. I enjoy helping others find their way when they are lost.

Example 2: A Coffee Mug
1. I am reliable and a part of people's morning routines.
2. I can handle "hot" or stressful situations without breaking.

Example 3: A Multi-Tool
1. I am versatile and can adapt to many different tasks.
2. I am practical and prefer solving problems over talking about them.

SUMMARY

This course focuses on building public speaking confidence through four distinct speech types. The first major hurdle is the Introduction Speech, which asks you to use a physical object as a metaphor for your personality. Remember to focus on preparation and note-taking rather than memorization, and ensure all quizzes are completed directly within Canvas.
