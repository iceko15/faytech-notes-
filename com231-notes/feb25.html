Speech Preparation & Oral Citations

BIG IDEA

Effective public speaking requires distinct main points supported by credible evidence, seamless transitions that recap and forecast content, and strict adherence to oral citation requirements to ensure academic integrity and audience trust.

KEY TERMS

  • Main Point Distinction: Each point must be unique in nature. If content overlaps (e.g., "I am nice" vs "I am kind"), they are not distinct and should be combined or revised.
  • Sufficient Evidence: The use of statistics, testimony, anecdotes, examples, comparisons, or images to support a claim.
  • Oral Citation: The act of verbally stating your source's credentials (author, publication, year) during the speech.
  • Smooth Transition: A bridge between points that recaps where the speech was and tells the audience where it is going.

STEPS/PROCESS

Oral Citation Requirements

  1. Quantity: You must cite six (6) different sources orally for full credit.
    • 4 sources = 80% credit.
  2. Quality & Info: Provide the Publication, Author, and Year. If an author or year is unavailable, provide whatever information is available.
  3. Avoid Titles: Do not read out article titles as they are long and unwieldy. Only mention titles if the source is a book or a film.
"I need to hear six different sources come out of your mouth. I do not want to hear the title unless it is like a book or a film... article titles are long and unwieldy."

RULES/IDENTITIES

  • The "No Empty Point" Rule: There should not be a single main point that lacks at least one source within its sub-content. Every main point must provide data or information.
  • Transition Formula: Avoid "half-crack" transitions like "My next point is..." instead use the Recap/Forecast method.

EXAMPLE 1: Transitions

The "Smooth" Transition:
"Not only [Point A: ABC], but also [Point B: DEA]."

EXAMPLE 2: Oral Citations

Proper Citation: "According to a 2023 article in the New York Times by Jane Doe..."
Improper Citation: "According to the article 'Ten Ways to Save the Planet' found on the internet..." (Too long/contains title).

EXAMPLE 3: Slideshow & Tech Prep

  • Deadline: Slideshows and Outlines (with Reference Pages) are due by 9:00 a.m. Monday to Canvas.
  • Backup Plan: Email the slides to yourself, bring a laptop, and ensure permissions are set for Google Slides or Canva.
  • Double Authentication Warning: Don't get locked out because your phone is in the car and you can't verify your password.

PRACTICE PROBLEMS

  • Review your outline: Does every main point have a source?
  • Check your transitions: Do they recap and forecast?
  • Count your oral citations: Do you have six unique sources with Author, Date, and Publication?
  • Note-taking: Have you marked on your notes where you intend to click the slides?
"Your speeches start Monday. Everyone's outline with the reference page and slideshow are due by 9 a.m. Monday to Canvas. Get it. Got it. Good."

SUMMARY

Prepare for Monday by finalizing your six oral citations, ensuring your transitions are smooth, and submitting all materials by the 9 a.m. deadline. Have a backup plan for your technology to avoid authentication or permission issues during your presentation.
