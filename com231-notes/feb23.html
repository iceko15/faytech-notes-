Speech Topics & Visual Citations

BIG IDEA

The transition from broad interest to specific speech topics requires audience engagement and a clear understanding of what constitutes a "source" in a visual presentation. Direct oral citation of data-heavy visuals is mandatory for academic credit.

Professor's Anecdote: Being sick as a parent is a different level of struggle. When both parents are sick, it's a "total mom moment" of survival—sleeping on the floor and handling feedings every two hours because "he could never do what I'm doing right now."

KEY TERMS

  • Broad Topic: A general subject area (e.g., "plants") that lacks the specificity needed for a persuasive or informative speech.
  • Audience Analysis: Asking classmates what they want to learn to narrow down a topic effectively.
  • Orally Citing Visuals: The verbal acknowledgement of where a graph, table, or diagram originated during the delivery of the speech.
  • Informational vs. Decorative Images: Distinguishing between a "tiger" (just an image) and a "diagram" (provides specific data/mechanisms).

STEPS/PROCESS

Narrowing the Topic

  1. Identify a broad interest (e.g., plants).
  2. Consult the audience: Ask what they would actually find useful or interesting.
  3. Pivot to specifics: Instead of "plants," discuss "why some people are immune to poison ivy" or "how to prevent specific plant-based skin reactions."

Citing Visual Aids Orally

  1. Display the visual aid (graph, chart, or table).
  2. Explicitly state the source: "As you can see in this graph published by a member of the [Name] method..."
  3. Do not assume the professor will "look up there" and check the source on the slide. If you want credit, you must say it.

RULES/IDENTITIES

  • The "Just a Tiger" Rule: If it's just a general image (like a tiger, helicopter, or plane) used for aesthetic purposes, an oral citation isn't necessarily required.
  • The "Diagram" Exception: If you are pointing out specific things happening in an image or if it's a diagram/table/chart, it is a source and MUST be cited.
  • Submission Deadlines: Aim for Friday if you want the professor to review your work. Do not wait until Sunday night; the professor has a child and does not work much on weekends.

EXAMPLE 1

Topic Narrowing:
Student: "I want to talk about plants."
Professor: "That's a broad topic. Ask your classmates. Why do some folks get poison and some don't? I want to know too, because if I look at it, I get it."

EXAMPLE 2

Oral Citation Phrasing:
Correct: "As shown in this chart from the 2022 CDC report..."
Incorrect: [Just pointing at the screen without speaking the source name].

EXAMPLE 3

Visual Differentiation:
Scenario A: A picture of a helicopter to show what one looks like (No oral citation needed).
Scenario B: A diagram showing the internal mechanics of a helicopter rotor (Oral citation REQUIRED).

PRACTICE PROBLEMS

  • Identify a broad topic you are interested in and write three specific "audience-focused" sub-topics for it.
  • Practice saying this sentence aloud: "According to the data found in this graph from [Source Name], the trend is increasing."
  • Review your current slide deck: Does every chart have a corresponding verbal citation in your script?

SUMMARY

Don't be afraid to ask the audience for help narrowing a topic. When it comes to visual aids, distinguish between decorative images and informational data. If it’s a graph, chart, table, or diagram, you must cite it orally during your speech to receive credit. Aim to have review-ready material by Friday to avoid the "weekend blackout" of grading.
