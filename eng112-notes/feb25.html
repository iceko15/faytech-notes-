Feb 25: Attendance Philosophy & The Cursive Debate

BIG IDEA

The session transitioned from a pedagogical "speech" regarding adult responsibility and attendance in a post-COVID academic landscape to a specific discussion on the cultural and practical decline of cursive writing. The central theme explores how institutional mandates (like attendance) and traditional skills (like cursive) are being permanently altered by technology and shifting societal standards.

Professor's Speech: I treat you like adults. If you are sitting there playing a game on your phone but not bothering anyone, I’m not going to do anything. That is on you. But you have to ask yourself: why are you here? COVID changed the game permanently. Attendance mandates still exist, but the "precision" of attendance has shifted.

KEY TERMS

  • Attendance Mandate: The institutional requirement for professors to track student presence, despite shifting attitudes post-COVID.
  • Cursive Literacy: The ability to read and write in script, which is increasingly becoming a specialized skill rather than a general educational standard.
  • Instructional Default: The professor's philosophy of treating students as self-governing adults unless their behavior becomes "disruptive to the class."

2. Identification of Classroom Disconnect

The professor highlighted a trend of "disengaged attendance" where students are physically present but mentally occupied with phones or games. The protocol is to only intervene if "ridiculous questions" are asked that reveal a lack of attention, which the professor finds "irritating."

RULES/IDENTITIES

The Cursive Argument (Pro-Instruction):

  • Historical Access: Major historical documents (e.g., the Declaration of Independence) are written in cursive. Without the ability to read it, these documents become inaccessible in their original form.
  • Cognitive Development: While often removed from curriculum, proponents argue it aids in fine motor skills and different ways of processing language.

The Modern Counter-Argument (Against Cursive):

  • Readability: Cursive is often viewed as "not easy on the eyes" and harder to digest than print.
  • Professionalism: Some modern perspectives label cursive as looking "unprofessional" compared to clean, digital-standard print.

EXAMPLE 1

The Cursive Literacy Gap: The professor notes that many people today claim they literally "cannot read cursive" because it was never taught. While the professor learned to read it at age five—well before it was taught in school—there is a growing demographic of students and even their siblings who find script script illegible.

EXAMPLE 2

The Public School Shift: The professor expresses "great admiration" for public school teachers but acknowledges they are simply "doing what they're being told to do." This includes the removal of cursive from the curriculum because it is no longer deemed essential by educational boards.

EXAMPLE 3

Personal Default: The professor defaults to an "adult-to-adult" relationship. Example: If a student is texting or playing a game, the professor watches but does not address it unless it disrupts the flow of the lecture for others.

PRACTICE PROBLEMS

Scenario Professor's Reaction
Student playing a game silently on their phone. No action; treated as an adult choice.
Student asking "ridiculous questions" because they weren't paying attention. Publicly "called out" for being irritating.
Student unable to read a historical primary source. Used as evidence for the necessity of cursive education.

SUMMARY

The lecture emphasized that while the professor maintains a hands-off approach to classroom management (expecting students to be responsible for their own learning), there is a distinct concern regarding the loss of traditional skills like cursive. The removal of these skills from public schools is seen as "unfortunate," especially when it creates a barrier between the modern student and historical texts. The "stigma" against cursive—that it is unprofessional or difficult to read—is contested by the professor, who views it as a basic literacy requirement that is being phased out unnecessarily.
