Chemical Bonding & Lattice Energy

BIG IDEA

Chemical bonding is driven by the movement or sharing of electrons to achieve a stable octet. This lecture transitions from Chapter 7 trends into the mechanics of Ionic and Covalent bonding, focusing on the energy released during lattice formation and the spectrum of electron sharing (polarity).

KEY TERMS

  • Ionic Bonding: Electrostatic attraction between ions formed by the transfer of electrons from a metal (low ionization energy) to a non-metal (high electron affinity).
  • Lattice Energy: The energy released when gas-phase ions come together to form one mole of an ionic solid. It is also the energy required to pull the lattice apart.
  • Covalent Bonding: A chemical bond involving the sharing of electron pairs between atoms, where the attractive force between nuclei and electrons outweighs the repulsive forces.
  • Electronegativity: The ability of an atom in a molecule to attract shared electrons to itself.
  • Dipole: A molecule or bond with two poles—one partial positive and one partial negative—due to unequal electron sharing.

STEPS/PROCESS

Predicting Ionic Charges

  1. Identify the element's group to find the number of valence electrons.
  2. Apply the Octet Rule: Metals lose electrons to reach the previous shell's octet; non-metals gain electrons to fill the current shell.
  3. Write the ion: Metals become cations ($+$), non-metals become anions ($-$).

Determining Bond Type via Electronegativity ($\Delta EN$)

  • $0.0$ to $0.4$: Non-polar Covalent (Equal sharing).
  • $0.4$ to $1.8$: Polar Covalent (Unequal sharing).
  • $> 1.8$: Ionic (Electron transfer).

RULES/IDENTITIES

Coulomb’s Law in Lattices

The force ($F$) and energy of the bond are governed by:

$$F = k \frac{Q_1 Q_2}{d^2}$$

Where $Q$ represents the charges and $d$ is the distance between nuclei.

  • Higher charges ($+2/-2$ vs $+1/-1$) lead to much higher lattice energy.
  • Smaller ions (shorter distance $d$) lead to higher lattice energy.

The Born-Haber Cycle

A state-function approach to calculating lattice energy by breaking the process into steps: sublimation of metal $\rightarrow$ dissociation of non-metal $\rightarrow$ ionization of metal $\rightarrow$ electron affinity of non-metal $\rightarrow$ formation of lattice.

EXAMPLE 1: Ionic Formation (Mg + S)

Magnesium (Group 2, 2 valence electrons) and Sulfur (Group 16, 6 valence electrons):

  • Mg loses 2 electrons $\rightarrow Mg^{2+}$
  • S gains 2 electrons $\rightarrow [S]^{2-}$ (with 8 dots in brackets)
  • Equation: $Mg: + \cdot \ddot{S} \cdot \rightarrow Mg^{2+} + [:\ddot{S}:]^{2-}$

EXAMPLE 2: Lattice Energy Ranking

Rank by increasing lattice energy: $CsI, NaCl, MgO, AlN$.

  1. CsI: $+1/-1$ charges, very large ions (long distance). Lowest energy.
  2. NaCl: $+1/-1$ charges, smaller ions than CsI.
  3. MgO: $+2/-2$ charges, similar size to NaCl but higher $Q$.
  4. AlN: $+3/-3$ charges. Highest energy.

EXAMPLE 3: Bond Polarity Symbols

For Hydrogen Fluoride ($HF$):

  • $H$ (EN 2.1), $F$ (EN 4.0). $\Delta EN = 1.9$ (Polar/Ionic border).
  • Notation 1 (Arrow): $H \rightarrow F$ with a cross on the H side ($\mapsto$).
  • Notation 2 (Delta): $\delta^+ H - F \delta^-$

SUMMARY

Lattice energy is the "glue" of ionic compounds, maximized by high charges and small radii. Covalent bonds exist on a spectrum defined by electronegativity; Fluorine is the king of pulling electrons. We use $\delta$ and dipole arrows to visualize this "tug-of-war" in polar molecules.

Professor's Sidebar: Fritz Haber, the man behind the Born-Haber cycle, is a controversial figure. He developed artificial fertilizers that feed 50% of the world today, but also pioneered chemical warfare (chlorine gas) in WWI and Zyklon B. Chemistry has massive real-world consequences, both good and bad.
