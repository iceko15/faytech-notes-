BIG IDEA

The Bohr model is limited because it only accurately explains the hydrogen atom. Modern chemistry relies on Quantum Mechanics to describe electrons not as particles in fixed orbits, but as wave functions that represent the probability of finding an electron in a specific region of space (an orbital). Every electron in an atom has a unique set of four quantum numbers that act as its "address."

KEY TERMS

  • Wave Function ($\psi$): A mathematical function describing the position of an electron.
  • Probability Density ($\psi^2$): The square of the wave function; it tells us the likelihood of finding an electron in a certain area.
  • Orbital: A region of space (not a physical path) where there is a 90% probability of finding an electron.
  • Ground State: The lowest possible energy arrangement of electrons in an atom.
  • Degenerate Orbitals: Orbitals that have the same energy level (e.g., the three different $2p$ orbitals).
Schrödinger's Equation: You don't need to solve this! It requires advanced calculus and linear algebra (Senior-level Chem major stuff). Just know that solving it gives us the wave function. $$ \hat{H}\psi = E\psi $$ The symbol $\psi$ is the Greek letter "psi." The symbol $\hbar$ (H-bar) is $h / 2\pi$.

THE FOUR QUANTUM NUMBERS

Name Symbol Definition Possible Values
Principal $n$ Distance from nucleus / Energy level $1, 2, 3, \dots, 7$
Angular Momentum $l$ Shape of the orbital $0$ to $n-1$
Magnetic $m_l$ Orientation in 3D space ($x, y, z$) $-l$ to $+l$
Spin $m_s$ Direction of electron spin $+1/2$ (up), $-1/2$ (down)

  • $l=0$: s (Spherical). Only one orientation ($m_l = 0$).

RULES FOR ELECTRONS

The Aufbau Principle (The Filling Order)

Electrons fill the lowest energy levels first before moving to higher ones.

[ Placeholder: Insert Diagonal Rule Arrow Diagram Here ]

PRO-TIP: Notice the arrow hits 4s BEFORE 3d. This is the #1 mistake on the test!

Why the 4s/3d Overlap?

As principal energy levels ($n$) increase, they get closer together. The $4s$ subshell is actually "lower" in energy than the $3d$ subshell, making it more stable to fill first.

Pauli Exclusion Principle: No two electrons in the same atom can have the exact same set of four quantum numbers. If they are in the same orbital (same $n, l, m_l$), they must have opposite spins ($m_s$).

Hund's Rule (The Bus Seat Rule): For degenerate orbitals (like the three $p$ boxes), electrons will fill each empty box with a "spin up" arrow first before they start pairing up with "spin down" arrows.

MAGNETIC PROPERTIES

Paramagnetism: At least one unpaired electron (attracted to magnets).

Diamagnetism: All electrons are paired (not attracted to magnets).

Link to Hund's Rule: Because we fill empty seats first, many atoms start off as paramagnetic.

DETERMINING ALLOWED QUANTUM NUMBERS

To check if a set is allowed, follow the chain: $n \rightarrow l \rightarrow m_l$.

  • If $n=2$, $l$ can only be $0$ or $1$. ($l=2$ is not allowed).
  • If $l=1$, $m_l$ can only be $-1, 0, +1$. ($m_l=2$ is not allowed).

The Quantum Address Checklist

  1. Step 1: Is $n$ a whole number (1-7)?
  2. Step 2: Is $l < n$? (If $n=3$, $l$ cannot be 3).
  3. Step 3: Is the absolute value of $m_l \le l$? (If $l=1$, $m_l$ cannot be 2).
  4. Step 4: Is $m_s$ exactly $+1/2$ or $-1/2$?

CORE VS. VALENCE ELECTRONS

  • Valence Electrons: Electrons in the highest principal energy level ($n$). These do all the bonding and reacting.
  • Core Electrons: All inner electrons. They are "shielded" by the valence electrons.

For Oxygen ($1s^2 2s^2 2p^4$), the highest $n$ is 2. There are 2 electrons in $2s$ and 4 in $2p$. Total valence = 6. Core = 2.

The 4s/3d Exception: Energy levels overlap. The $4s$ orbital actually fills before the $3d$ orbital because it is slightly lower in energy.

EXAMPLE: TITANIUM (Ti, 22 Electrons)

1. $1s^2$ (2 total)

2. $2s^2$ (4 total)

3. $2p^6$ (10 total)

4. $3s^2$ (12 total)

5. $3p^6$ (18 total)

6. $4s^2$ (20 total) — Fills before 3d!

7. $3d^2$ (22 total)

Full Configuration: $1s^2 2s^2 2p^6 3s^2 3p^6 4s^2 3d^2$

Classroom Note: Instructor's Tip: For transition metals (the d-block), valence electron rules get tricky. Focus on the full configuration for now!

NOBLE GAS SHORTHAND

To save time and focus on the valence electrons, we can use the Noble Gas from the previous row in brackets to represent the core electrons.

Example: For Titanium, instead of writing the full $1s^2 2s^2 2p^6 3s^2 3p^6 4s^2 3d^2$, we can replace the first 18 electrons with the symbol for Argon ($Ar$).

Shorthand Configuration: $[Ar] 4s^2 3d^2$

THE STABILITY EXCEPTION: CR & CU

Half-filled ($d^5$) and fully-filled ($d^{10}$) subshells are exceptionally stable. Because the $4s$ and $3d$ energy levels are so close together, an electron will promote from the $4s$ orbital to the $3d$ orbital to achieve this stability.

Chromium ($Z=24$):
  • Expected: $[Ar] 4s^2 3d^4$
  • Actual: $[Ar] 4s^1 3d^5$
Copper ($Z=29$):
  • Expected: $[Ar] 4s^2 3d^9$
  • Actual: $[Ar] 4s^1 3d^{10}$

SUMMARY

Modern atomic theory moves away from circular "orbits" to 3D "orbitals." By using $n, l, m_l,$ and $m_s$, we can pinpoint the highest probability regions for every electron in an atom. Next class, we will learn how to use the periodic table as a shortcut to skip memorizing the filling order. Understanding these rules explains the physical properties of elements, like their magnetism and how they bond.

PRACTICE CHECK

  1. Is the set $n=3, l=3, m_l=0$ allowed?
    No, $l$ must be strictly less than $n$ (so for $n=3$, $l$ can only be up to 2).
  2. How many valence electrons does Carbon ($1s^2 2s^2 2p^2$) have?
    4
  3. Which fills first: 3p or 4s?
    3p
  4. Is $[Ar] 4s^2 3d^4$ the correct ground-state configuration for Chromium?
    No, it is $[Ar] 4s^1 3d^5$ because a half-filled $d^5$ subshell is exceptionally stable.
