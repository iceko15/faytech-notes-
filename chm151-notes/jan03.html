Aqueous Solutions & the pH Scale

BIG IDEA

A solution's acidity or alkalinity is determined by the concentration of specific ions within the solvent. The pH scale is logarithmic and inverse: a high concentration of \(H^+\) ions results in a low pH value.

KEY TERMS

Term Definition
Solute The substance being dissolved (e.g., the acid or base added to the flask).
Solvent The dissolving medium (in aqueous solutions, this is water).
Hydronium (\(H^+\)) The ion responsible for acidic properties.
Hydroxide (\(OH^-\)) The ion responsible for basic (alkaline) properties.

STEPS/PROCESS: TITRATION COMPONENTS

  1. The Setup: Identify the solvent (lining the flask) and the solute (the functional substance).
  2. Ion Check: Determine if the solution is dominated by \(H^+\) or \(OH^-\).
  3. Molarity Calculation: Determine the concentration (moles/liter) to find the weight of the concentration.
  4. pH Mapping: Translate the ion concentration to the 0–14 scale.

RULES: THE pH QUADRANTS

  • Acidic Solutions: High \(H^+\) concentration = Low pH (0–6). Example: Stomach acid is roughly 2.
  • Neutral Solutions: Equal amounts of \(H^+\) and \(OH^-\) = pH of 7.
  • Basic Solutions: High \(OH^-\) concentration = High pH (8–14).
  • Concentration Paradox: "High on the tail" means basic; don't confuse "ton of ions" with a "high number" on the scale for acids.

EXAMPLE/DEMONSTRATION

1. The Beaker Quadrants

Imagine a beaker during a titration:
  • Solvent: The water volume filling the flask.
  • Solute: The concentrated acid drops touching the liquid.

2. Logarithmic Logic

If solution A has a pH of 2 and solution B has a pH of 3, solution A is 10 times more acidic than solution B because it has a higher concentration of \(H^+\).

3. Identifying a Base

If a solution contains a high amount of \(OH^-\) (Hydroxide), it will be found on the high end of the scale (e.g., pH 12 or 13).

PRACTICE PROBLEMS

  1. If a solution is "low on the pH tail" (e.g., pH 1), is it acidic or basic? (Answer: Acidic)
  2. Which ion is present in high concentrations in a basic solution? (Answer: \(OH^-\))
  3. What is the pH of a neutral solution where \(H^+\) and \(OH^-\) are equal? (Answer: 7)

SUMMARY

In the lab, always distinguish between the solute and the solvent. When measuring acidity, remember the inverse rule: a high density of Hydrogen ions (\(H^+\)) correlates to a low pH number, while Hydroxide ions (\(OH^-\)) dominate the high, basic end of the scale.
