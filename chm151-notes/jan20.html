Atomic Structure, Periodic Table Layout, and Formulas

BIG IDEA

Elements are defined by their number of protons (Atomic Number). The Periodic Table organizes these elements so that those with similar chemical and physical properties fall into the same columns (Groups). Understanding the difference between individual isotope mass numbers and the weighted average atomic weight is crucial for chemical calculations.

KEY TERMS

  • Atomic Number ($Z$): The number of protons in the nucleus; the identifying factor of an element.
  • Mass Number ($A$): The sum of protons + neutrons in a specific isotope.
  • Isotope: Atoms of the same element (same protons) with different numbers of neutrons.
  • Ion: An atom with an imbalance of protons and electrons, resulting in a net charge.
    • Cation: Positively charged ion (metals).
    • Anion: Negatively charged ion (non-metals).
  • Periods: Horizontal rows (1-7).
  • Groups: Vertical columns (1-18). Elements in a group share similar properties.
  • Atomic Mass Unit (AMU): $1 \text{ AMU} = 1.6 \times 10^{-24} \text{ grams}$.
  • Atomic Weight: The weighted average of all naturally occurring isotopes of an element.

STEPS/PROCESS: Writing Chemical Symbols

When writing an isotopic symbol for an ion, use the following placement:

$$_Z^A \text{X}^{\text{charge}}$$
  1. $\text{X}$: Element Symbol (Determined by protons).
  2. $A$: Mass Number (Protons + Neutrons) - Top Left.
  3. $Z$: Atomic Number (Protons) - Bottom Left.
  4. Charge: (Protons - Electrons) - Top Right. If neutral, leave blank.

RULES/IDENTITIES

1. The "Must-Know" Elements

You must know the name and symbol link for the first 36 elements, plus these extras:

  • Silver (Ag), Gold (Au), Mercury (Hg), Lead (Pb), and Iodine (I).

2. Classification of Elements

Type Properties Location
Metals Shiny, conduct heat/electricity, solid at room temp (except Mercury/Hg). Left of the zigzag line.
Non-metals Dull, poor conductors (insulators), vary in phase (solid, gas, one liquid: Bromine/Br). Right of the zigzag line.
Metalloids Semi-conductors; have properties of both metals and non-metals. Touching the zigzag line.
Exception: Hydrogen (H) is the "weird" exception. It is on the left side but behaves mostly as a non-metal. It can behave as both.

3. Named Groups

  • Group 1: Alkali Metals (Very reactive with water; reactivity increases down the group).
  • Group 2: Alkaline Earth Metals (Used in fireworks to change flame colors).
  • Groups 3-12: Transition Metals (Variable charges).
  • Group 17: Halogens (Very reactive; mostly gases except Br).
  • Group 18: Noble Gases (Inert/Unreactive; glow when electric current is applied).

4. Diatomic Molecules (The "7" Rule)

Seven elements never exist as single atoms in nature; they are homonuclear diatomic ($X_2$):

  • Hydrogen ($H_2$), Nitrogen ($N_2$), Oxygen ($O_2$), Fluorine ($F_2$), Chlorine ($Cl_2$), Bromine ($Br_2$), Iodine ($I_2$).
  • Mnemonic: "BrINClHOF" or the "Shape of a 7" on the table starting at Nitrogen plus Hydrogen.

EXAMPLE 1: Identifying an Ion

Given: 8 Protons, 9 Neutrons, 9 Electrons.

  1. Element: 8 Protons = Atomic Number 8 = Oxygen (O).
  2. Mass Number: $8 \text{ protons} + 9 \text{ neutrons} = 17$.
  3. Atomic Number: 8.
  4. Charge: $8(+) + 9(-) = -1$.

Result: $_{8}^{17}\text{O}^{-}$

EXAMPLE 2: Determining Abundance

Boron has two isotopes: Boron-10 ($10.013 \text{ AMU}$) and Boron-11 ($11.009 \text{ AMU}$). The periodic table lists Boron's atomic weight as $10.81$. Which is more abundant?

  • Logic: $10.81$ is much closer to $11$ than to $10$. If it were 50/50, the weight would be $10.5$.
  • Conclusion: Boron-11 is significantly more abundant in nature.

Molecular Formula (Exact) Empirical Formula (Simplified Ratio)
$C_2H_4$ $CH_2$ (Divide by 2)
$B_2H_6$ $BH_3$ (Divide by 2)
$H_2O$ $H_2O$ (Already lowest ratio)
$C_4H_{14}O_2$ $C_2H_7O$ (Divide by 2)

SUMMARY

We use Mass Spectrometry to determine the percentages of isotopes in a sample. This data allows us to calculate the Atomic Weight shown on the Periodic Table. In chemical notation, the molecular formula shows the actual number of atoms in a molecule, while the empirical formula shows the simplest whole-number ratio.
