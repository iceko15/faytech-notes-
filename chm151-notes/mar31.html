BIG IDEA

Understanding how electrons are distributed within molecules. We use electronegativity to determine if bonds are polar or ionic, and Lewis Structures to visualize the connectivity and valence electron distribution in covalent compounds.

KEY TERMS

  • Electronegativity: How strongly an atom pulls electrons toward itself within a bond.
  • Bonding Pair: Two electrons shared between atoms (represented by a line).
  • Lone Pair: A pair of valence electrons not involved in bonding (represented by dots).
  • Terminal Atom: An atom located on the outside of a structure (not the center).
  • Formal Charge: A bookkeeping method to track electrons; it helps identify the most "dominant" or stable Lewis structure.

RULES/IDENTITIES

Bond Polarity Based on $\Delta EN$

Electronegativity Difference ($\Delta EN$) Bond Type
$0$ to $0.4$ Non-polar Covalent
$0.4$ to $1.8$ Polar Covalent
Greater than $1.8$ Ionic

Steps to Building Lewis Structures

  1. Calculate Total Valence Electrons: Sum valence electrons for all atoms. For anions, add electrons (e.g., -1 charge = +1e). For cations, subtract electrons.
  2. Identify Central Atom: Usually the least electronegative atom. Exception: Hydrogen can NEVER be the central atom.
  3. Connect Atoms: Draw single bonds (lines) from the central atom to terminal atoms.
  4. Fill Octets (Outside In): Add lone pairs to terminal atoms first to reach 8 electrons (except Hydrogen, which only needs 2).
  5. Place Remaining Electrons: Put any leftover electrons on the central atom.
  6. Check Octets/Form Multiple Bonds: If the central atom lacks an octet, move lone pairs from terminal atoms to form double or triple bonds.
Dr. A's Tip: Use the "tends to make" pattern. Atoms usually form a number of bonds equal to the electrons they need to reach an octet. Carbon (4 valence) tends to make 4 bonds. Nitrogen (5 valence) tends to make 3. Oxygen (6 valence) tends to make 2.

EXAMPLE 1: $PCl_3$

  1. Valence: $P (5) + Cl (7 \times 3) = 26$ total electrons.
  2. Center: $P$ (less electronegative than $Cl$).
  3. Single bonds to each $Cl$ uses 6 electrons ($26 - 6 = 20$ left).
  4. Fill $Cl$ octets: 6 dots on each $Cl$ uses 18 electrons ($20 - 18 = 2$ left).
  5. Remaining 2 electrons go on $P$ as a lone pair.

EXAMPLE 2: Formal Charge Determination ($CO_2$)

Formula for Formal Charge: $$FC = (\text{Valence } e^-) - (\text{Lone pair } e^-) - \frac{1}{2}(\text{Bonding } e^-)$$

Structure Choice for $CO_2$ (16 electrons total):

  • Option A: $O=C=O$ (Double bonds on both sides)
    • Oxygen: $6 - 4 - 2 = 0$
    • Carbon: $4 - 0 - 4 = 0$
    • Dominant structure (all charges 0).
  • Option B: $O \equiv C - O$ (Triple bond and single bond)
    • Triple-bonded Oxygen: $6 - 2 - 3 = +1$
    • Single-bonded Oxygen: $6 - 6 - 1 = -1$
    • Carbon: $4 - 0 - 4 = 0$

EXAMPLE 3: $NCS^-$ (Thiocyanate Ion)

Total Valence: $N(5) + C(4) + S(6) + 1(\text{charge}) = 16$. Comparing structures via Formal Charge:
  1. $[N \equiv C - S]^-$: $N(0), C(0), S(-1)$
  2. $[N = C = S]^-$: $N(-1), C(0), S(0)$
  3. $[N - C \equiv S]^-$: $N(-2), C(0), S(+1)$
Dominant Structure: Structure 2 ($[N=C=S]^-$). Why? Charges are closer to zero than Structure 3, and the negative formal charge resides on Nitrogen, which is more electronegative than Sulfur.
Shortcut for Formal Charge: Count "things" around the atom (dots + lines). Subtract that from the valence number. If the atom has more things than its valence number, it's negative. If fewer, it's positive.

SUMMARY

  • $\Delta EN$ determines if electrons are shared equally, unequally, or transferred entirely.
  • Dipoles point toward the more electronegative atom (only in polar covalent bonds).
  • Lewis structures must show all valence electrons and prioritize octets.
  • Charged molecules MUST be shown in brackets with the charge indicated in the top right.
  • The most stable structure has formal charges closest to zero and negative charges on the most electronegative elements.
