BIG IDEA

Concentration (Molarity) can be calculated for an entire compound or for specific individual ions within that compound. Furthermore, concentrated "Stock Solutions" can be diluted to lower concentrations by adding solvent, a process governed by the conservation of moles.

KEY TERMS

  • Molarity ($M$): Defined as moles of solute per liter of solution ($M = \frac{mol}{L}$).
  • Stock Solution: A concentrated form of a solution kept in stock to be diluted for later use.
  • Dilution: The process of adding more solvent (usually water) to decrease the concentration without changing the total moles of solute.
  • Volumetric Flask: A specialized piece of glassware with a precise calibration line used to prepare solutions of a specific volume (e.g., 50 mL, 25 mL).

CALCULATING ION CONCENTRATION

When an ionic compound dissolves, it may release multiple ions per formula unit. We use subscripts to determine the mole ratio of ions to the total compound.

Example: Potassium Sulfate ($K_2SO_4$)

When $K_2SO_4$ dissolves: $K_2SO_4(aq) \rightarrow 2K^+(aq) + SO_4^{2-}(aq)$

  • There are twice as many Potassium ions ($K^+$) as there are Sulfate ions ($SO_4^{2-}$).
  • If the concentration of the compound is $1.0 M$, the concentration of $K^+$ is $2.0 M$.

EXAMPLE 1: Sodium Phosphate Ion Concentration

Problem: Calculate the molar concentration of Sodium ions ($Na^+$) formed from 0.50 moles of Sodium Phosphate ($Na_3PO_4$) dissolved in enough water to make 1.0 Liter of solution.

Step 1: Determine the Formula. Sodium is $Na^+$ (Group 1). Phosphate is $PO_4^{3-}$. The formula is $Na_3PO_4$.

Step 2: Find the Mole Ratio. For every 1 mole of $Na_3PO_4$, there are 3 moles of $Na^+$.

Step 3: Convert Moles of Compound to Moles of Ion.

$$0.50 \text{ mol } Na_3PO_4 \times \frac{3 \text{ mol } Na^+}{1 \text{ mol } Na_3PO_4} = 1.50 \text{ mol } Na^+$$

Step 4: Calculate Molarity.

$$M = \frac{1.50 \text{ mol } Na^+}{1.0 \text{ L}} = 1.50 M \text{ of } Na^+$$

THE DILUTION EQUATION

Since the number of moles of solute does not change during dilution (you are only adding water), the initial moles must equal the final moles.

$$M_1V_1 = M_2V_2$$

  • $M_1$: Initial Molarity (Stock)
  • $V_1$: Initial Volume (How much stock you need to pull)
  • $M_2$: Final Molarity (Desired)
  • $V_2$: Final Volume (Total volume after adding water)
Critical Distinction: Use $M_1V_1 = M_2V_2$ only for dilution (taking the same substance and making it weaker). If a chemical reaction is occurring (e.g., reacting $HCl$ with $NaOH$), you cannot use this equation; you must use Stoichiometry.

EXAMPLE 2: Dilution with Ion Concentration

Problem: Calculate the volume (mL) of $4.0 M$ $K_2SO_4$ needed to prepare $600$ mL of $0.040 M$ $K^+$ solution.

Step 1: Normalize the Units. The stock solution is given in terms of the compound ($K_2SO_4$), but the goal is in terms of the ion ($K^+$). We must find the initial concentration of $K^+$.

Because there are 2 $K$ per $K_2SO_4$, the stock concentration of $K^+$ is: $$4.0 M \times 2 = 8.0 M \text{ (This is our } M_1)$$

Step 2: Identify Variables.

  • $M_1 = 8.0 M$
  • $V_1 = ?$
  • $M_2 = 0.040 M$
  • $V_2 = 600 \text{ mL}$

Step 3: Solve for $V_1$.

$$V_1 = \frac{M_2 \times V_2}{M_1}$$ $$V_1 = \frac{0.040 M \times 600 \text{ mL}}{8.0 M} = 3.00 \text{ mL}$$

Meaning: You take 3 mL of stock and add 597 mL of water to reach the 600 mL mark.

PRACTICE PROBLEM

Problem: Calculate the volume of $5.0 M$ $K_2SO_4$ needed to prepare $800$ mL of a $0.120 M$ solution of $K_2SO_4$.

Solution: Since both concentrations are for the same compound ($K_2SO_4$), no ion-ratio adjustment is needed.

$$M_1 = 5.0 M, M_2 = 0.120 M, V_2 = 800 \text{ mL}$$ $$V_1 = \frac{0.120 M \times 800 \text{ mL}}{5.0 M} = 19.2 \text{ mL}$$

SUMMARY

  • Concentration depends on the species defined (compound vs. individual ions).
  • Preparing a solution involves weighing solute, placing it in a volumetric flask, and filling to the volume line.
  • Adding water increases volume and decreases concentration but leaves total moles unchanged.
