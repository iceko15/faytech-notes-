CHM-151: Periodic Trends & Ionization

BIG IDEA

The core concept of Chapter 7 is Effective Nuclear Charge ($Z_{eff}$). This is the net positive charge (pull) felt by valence electrons from the nucleus. This pull is determined by the number of protons versus the shielding effect of core electrons. $Z_{eff}$ dictates nearly every physical trend: atomic size, ion size, and the energy required to remove or add electrons.

KEY TERMS

  • Effective Nuclear Charge ($Z_{eff}$): The amount of "pull" outer electrons feel from the nucleus after core shielding is accounted for.
  • Isoelectronic Series: A group of atoms/ions that all have the same number of electrons (e.g., $O^{2-}$, $F^-$, $Ne$, $Na^+$, $Mg^{2+}$, $Al^{3+}$ all have 10 electrons).
  • Ionization Energy (IE): The energy required to remove an electron from a gaseous atom or ion.
  • Electron Affinity (EA): The energy change (usually energy released) when an electron is added to a neutral atom.
  • Octet Rule: The tendency of atoms to prefer a configuration of 8 valence electrons.

STEPS/PROCESS

Explaining Trends using Coulomb’s Law ($F = k \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}$):

  1. Left to Right: Shielding remains constant while protons increase. This increases $Q$ (charge), making the pull stronger. Result: Smaller atoms, higher Ionization Energy.
  2. Top to Bottom: We are adding energy levels, which increases $r$ (distance). Distance has a massive impact on force. Result: Larger atoms, lower Ionization Energy.
  3. Transition Metals: When forming ions, transition metals lose their $s$ electrons before their $d$ electrons because the $s$ shell is physically further from the nucleus.

RULES/IDENTITIES

Trend Moving Left to Right Moving Top to Bottom
Effective Nuclear Charge ($Z_{eff}$) Increases Decreases
Atomic Radius Decreases (gets smaller) Increases (gets larger)
Ionization Energy Increases Decreases
Cation Size Smaller than neutral parent Increases
Anion Size Larger than neutral parent Increases
Exam Hint: Why do anions get bigger? It’s not just the extra electron; it’s the electron repulsion. More negative charges in the same space force them to spread out further.

EXAMPLE 1: Ranking Neutral Size

Task: Rank from smallest to largest size: $Si$, $Al$, $Ga$.
1. $Si$ is furthest up and right. It is the smallest.
2. $Al$ is to the left of $Si$ in the same period. It is larger than $Si$.
3. $Ga$ is below $Al$. Because distance increases with the next energy level, $Ga$ is the largest.
Order: $Si < Al < Ga$

EXAMPLE 2: Isoelectronic Series Size

Task: Rank $O^{2-}$, $F^-$, $Ne$, $Na^+$, $Mg^{2+}$, $Al^{3+}$.
All have 10 electrons. We must look at the number of protons (the "magnet" in the center).
* $Al^{3+}$ has 13 protons pulling on 10 electrons. (Strongest pull = Smallest)
* $O^{2-}$ has only 8 protons pulling on 10 electrons. (Weakest pull = Largest)
Order (Smallest to Largest): $Al^{3+} < Mg^{2+} < Na^+ < Ne < F^- < O^{2-}$

EXAMPLE 3: Successive Ionization Energies

Task: Why is the $IE_2$ of Lithium ($7298 kJ/mol$) so much higher than $IE_1$ ($520 kJ/mol$)?
* $IE_1$ removes a valence electron ($2s^1$).
* $IE_2$ attempts to remove a core electron ($1s^2$).
Core electrons are closer to the nucleus and feel the full force of the protons without valence shielding. This "jump" in energy indicates you have hit the core.

PRACTICE PROBLEMS

1. Which has higher IE: $Ca$ or $As$?
$As$ is further right. Higher $Z_{eff}$ means it holds electrons tighter. Result: $As$.

2. Which is smaller: $Fe^{2+}$ or $Fe^{3+}$?
$Fe^{3+}$ has a higher positive charge, meaning the same number of protons are pulling on fewer electrons. Result: $Fe^{3+}$.

3. Configuration for $Fe^{2+}$:
Neutral $Fe$: $[Ar] 4s^2 3d^6$. Remove $4s$ first. Result: $[Ar] 3d^6$.

SUMMARY

Everything comes back to how tight the nucleus holds the electrons. Smaller atoms hold electrons tighter, making them harder to remove (High IE). Larger atoms hold them loosely (Low IE). Non-metals love to gain electrons (High Electron Affinity) to reach an octet, while metals prefer to lose electrons to reach a stable previous-shell configuration.
