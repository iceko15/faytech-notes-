Chapter 6: Quantum Theory and Electronic Structure

BIG IDEA: Explores the dual nature of light and matter, the quantum mechanical model of the atom, and the rules governing electron configurations (n, l, ml, ms).

Chapter Summary: Key Equations

  • Speed of Light: $c = \lambda\nu$
  • Energy of a Photon: $E = h\nu$ or $E = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$
  • de Broglie Wavelength: $\lambda = \frac{h}{mv}$
  • Rydberg Equation: $\Delta E = R_H \left( \frac{1}{n_i^2} - \frac{1}{n_f^2} \right)$

Constants:

  • $c = 3.00 \times 10^8 \, \text{m/s}$
  • $h = 6.63 \times 10^{-34} \, \text{J}\cdot\text{s}$
  • $R_H = 2.18 \times 10^{-18} \, \text{J}$

6.1 The Nature of Light

Electromagnetic radiation carries energy through space and exhibits wave-like behavior. We define waves using three primary properties:

  • Wavelength ($\lambda$): The distance between two consecutive peaks or troughs in a wave.
  • Frequency ($\nu$): The number of wave cycles that pass a specific point in one second (measured in Hertz, Hz or $s^{-1}$).
  • Amplitude: The vertical distance from the midline of a wave to the peak or trough (related to intensity/brightness).
Speed of Light Equation: $$c = \lambda\nu$$ Where $c = 3.00 \times 10^8 \, \text{m/s}$ (a constant in a vacuum).

Unit Conversion Cheat Sheet

Light calculations often require converting units before plugging them into equations. Here are the most common conversions you will need:

  • Nanometers to Meters: $1 \, \text{nm} = 1 \times 10^{-9} \, \text{m}$
  • Angstroms to Meters: $1 \, \text{\AA} = 1 \times 10^{-10} \, \text{m}$
  • Megahertz to Hertz: $1 \, \text{MHz} = 1 \times 10^6 \, \text{Hz}$

Note: There is an inverse relationship between wavelength and frequency. Higher frequency equals shorter wavelength.

6.2 Quantum Theory

Classical physics assumed energy was continuous. Max Planck discovered that energy can only be gained or lost in whole-number multiples of the quantity $h\nu$.

Planck's Equation: $$E = h\nu$$ Where $h = 6.63 \times 10^{-34} \, \text{J}\cdot\text{s}$ (Planck's Constant).

Context & Implementation: Classical physics viewed energy like a ramp (continuous), but Planck proved it behaves like a staircase (quantized). We use $E = h\nu$ to find the energy of a single photon. If a problem asks for the energy of a 'mole' of photons, multiply the result by Avogadro's Number ($6.022 \times 10^{23}$).

The 'In-a-Nutshell' Energy Guide

  • $E \uparrow$ as $\nu \uparrow$ (Directly proportional)
  • $E \uparrow$ as $\lambda \downarrow$ (Inversely proportional)
  • $\text{Higher } n \rightarrow \text{Larger Orbital } \rightarrow \text{Higher Energy}$

Deep Dive: Instructors often use the 'Blackbody Radiation' or 'Ultraviolet Catastrophe' as the reason why classical physics failed. The key takeaway is that energy is 'discontinuous' - it moves in specific steps (quanta).

6.3 The Photoelectric Effect

Albert Einstein proposed that electromagnetic radiation can be viewed as a stream of particles called photons. This explained why light requires a minimum frequency to eject electrons from a metal surface.

Photon Energy Calculation: $$E_{\text{photon}} = h\nu = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$$

Context & Implementation: Einstein used Planck's 'packets' to show that light isn't just a wave; it hits metal like a physical pool ball (photon). If the pool ball doesn't have enough speed (threshold frequency), the electron won't budge. Increasing light intensity only adds more photons; it doesn't make the individual photons stronger. Only changing the frequency/color makes them stronger.

6.4 Bohr's Theory of the Hydrogen Atom

Niels Bohr proposed that electrons exist in specific orbits around the nucleus, and these orbits correspond to specific quantized energy levels.

  • Ground State ($n=1$): The lowest energy state of an atom.
  • Excited State ($n > 1$): Any energy state higher than the ground state.
Rydberg Equation ($\Delta E$): $$\Delta E = R_H \left( \frac{1}{n_i^2} - \frac{1}{n_f^2} \right)$$ Where $R_H = 2.18 \times 10^{-18} \, \text{J}$ (Rydberg Constant).

Context & Implementation: Bohr's model was the first to show that electrons don't just float - they are locked into 'neighborhoods' (shells). If an electron 'falls' from $n=3$ to $n=1$, energy is released (emission). If it 'jumps' from $n=1$ to $n=3$, energy is absorbed.

Deep Dive: Note that Bohr's model ONLY perfectly predicts the Hydrogen atom. It fails for anything with more than one electron. The 'Line Spectrum' is like an atom's fingerprint; no two elements emit the exact same colors of light.

6.5 Wave Properties of Matter

Louis de Broglie hypothesized that if light can behave like a particle, then matter (such as an electron) can behave like a wave.

de Broglie Equation: $$\lambda = \frac{h}{mv}$$ Where $\lambda$ is wavelength, $h$ is Planck's constant, $m$ is mass (kg), and $v$ is velocity (m/s).

Context & Implementation: This concept introduces 'Wave-Particle Duality' - everything has wave properties, but large objects (like a baseball) have wavelengths too small to see. Electrons are small enough that their 'waviness' matters significantly.

6.6 The Uncertainty Principle

Werner Heisenberg determined that it is impossible to know simultaneously both the momentum ($p$) and the position ($x$) of a particle with absolute certainty.

Heisenberg's Uncertainty Principle: $$\Delta x \Delta p \geq \frac{h}{4\pi}$$

Context & Implementation: The act of measuring an electron's position actually bumps it, changing its momentum. This fundamental uncertainty is why we use 'Orbitals' (probability maps) instead of 'Orbits' (set paths).

6.7 Quantum Numbers

We describe an electron's "address" within an atom using four quantum numbers:

  • Principal Quantum Number ($n$): Describes the energy level and size. Values are integers $1, 2, 3...$
  • Angular Momentum Quantum Number ($\ell$): Describes the shape of the orbital. Values range from $0$ to $(n-1)$.
    • $\ell=0$ (s)
    • $\ell=1$ (p)
    • $\ell=2$ (d)
    • $\ell=3$ (f)
  • Magnetic Quantum Number ($m_\ell$): Describes the orientation in space. Values range from $-\ell$ to $+\ell$.
  • Spin Quantum Number ($m_s$): Describes the direction of electron spin. Values are $+1/2$ or $-1/2$.

Deep Dive: The 'Principal' (n) tells you the size, 'Angular' (l) tells you the shape, and 'Magnetic' (ml) tells you the orientation. If you hear 'Shell', they mean (n). If you hear 'Subshell', they mean (l).

6.8 Atomic Orbitals

The quantum numbers define the shapes of the orbitals where electrons are found:

  • s orbitals: Spherical.
  • p orbitals: Dumbbell-shaped (3 orientations: $x, y, z$).
  • d orbitals: Cloverleaf or "donut" shapes (5 orientations).

Visualizing the Blocks

The Periodic Table is organized into blocks that correspond to the last subshell being filled.

  • s-block: Groups 1, 2, and Helium (left side).
  • p-block: Groups 13-18 (right side).
  • d-block: Transition Metals (middle).
  • f-block: Lanthanides and Actinides (bottom).

Mission Note: The period number ($n$) matches the energy level for s and p blocks, but for the d-block, the energy level is ($n-1$).

Deep Dive: Pay attention to the 'Nodes' - these are areas where the probability of finding an electron is zero. The 'Probability Density' is what gives orbitals their spherical or dumbbell shapes.

6.9 Electron Configuration Rules

Three core rules govern how electrons fill these orbitals:

1. Pauli Exclusion Principle: No two electrons in an atom can have the same four quantum numbers. An orbital holds a maximum of 2 electrons with opposite spins.
2. Aufbau Principle: Electrons fill the lowest energy orbitals first.
3. Hund's Rule: Electrons occupy orbitals of the same energy singly with parallel spins before doubling up.

Electronic Structure: The Filling Order Guide

To determine the electron configuration of an atom, we follow the Aufbau Principle, which states that electrons fill the lowest energy orbitals first. This order is not strictly numerical.

The Diagonal Rule (Filling Order):
  • $1s \rightarrow 2s \rightarrow 2p \rightarrow 3s \rightarrow 3p$
  • Look Out: $\mathbf{4s \rightarrow 3d} \rightarrow 4p$
  • $5s \rightarrow 4d...$

Key Exception (4s before 3d): Even though the principal quantum number 4 is higher than 3, the $4s$ orbital is slightly lower in energy than the $3d$ orbital. Therefore, electrons fill the $4s$ orbital before they begin filling the $3d$ orbital.

Noble Gas Shorthand

Writing full configurations is slow. Use the previous Noble Gas to represent the core electrons.

  • Phosphorus (Full): $1s^2 2s^2 2p^6 3s^2 3p^3$
  • Phosphorus (Shorthand): $[Ne] 3s^2 3p^3$

Advanced Configuration Analysis

The Magnetism Test

How to tell if an atom will be attracted to a magnetic field.

  • Rule 1: Write the orbital diagram.
  • Rule 2: If there is at least ONE single (unpaired) electron, it is Paramagnetic.
  • Rule 3: If every single electron has a partner, it is Diamagnetic.

Mission-Critical Exceptions: Chromium and Copper

Chromium ($Cr$) and Copper ($Cu$) do not follow the standard Aufbau order because a half-filled or fully-filled d-subshell is more stable.

  • Chromium ($Z=24$): $[Ar] 4s^1 3d^5$ (Not $4s^2 3d^4$)
  • Copper ($Z=29$): $[Ar] 4s^1 3d^{10}$ (Not $4s^2 3d^9$)

Deep Dive: The Aufbau principle is the 'building up' process. Hund's rule is about 'maximizing spin' (the bus seat rule). The Pauli Exclusion Principle is why we draw one arrow up and one arrow down in a single box.
