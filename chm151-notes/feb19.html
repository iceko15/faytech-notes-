Feb 19: Systems, Surroundings, and Internal Energy

Exam Alert: The next test covers Chapters 4 and 5. It is two weeks from today. Chapter 5 is a bit longer, so we need to stay on track.

BIG IDEA

In thermodynamics, everything is analyzed from the perspective of the system. We must strictly define what constitutes the system versus the surroundings to determine the sign (positive or negative) of energy transfers like heat and work.

KEY TERMS

  • System: The specific part of the universe we are studying. In chemistry, this is almost always the reactants and products (the compounds) involved in a reaction.
  • Surroundings: Everything else in the universe outside the system. This includes the container (beaker, flask), the room, and the air around it.
  • Internal Energy ($E$): The sum of all kinetic and potential energies of all components of the system.
  • First Law of Thermodynamics: Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred. Mathematically: $\Delta E = q + w$.
  • Enthalpy ($H$): The heat content of a system at constant pressure.

STEPS/PROCESS

1. Identify the System and Surroundings

If hydrogen and oxygen react in a cylinder with a piston, the $H_2$ and $O_2$ molecules are the system. The cylinder, the piston, and the surrounding atmosphere are the surroundings.

2. Determine the Sign of Energy Transfer

Process Sign Definition
System gains heat $q > 0$ (+) Endothermic
System loses heat $q < 0$ (-) Exothermic
Work done ON system $w > 0$ (+) Surroundings push on system (Compression)
Work done BY system $w < 0$ (-) System pushes on surroundings (Expansion)

RULES/IDENTITIES

  • The Change in Internal Energy: $\Delta E = E_{final} - E_{initial}$
  • The Fundamental Equation: $\Delta E = q + w$
  • Relationship to Enthalpy: At constant pressure, $\Delta H = q_p$.
Professor's Note on Work: Think of work as effort. If the system is doing the work (pushing a piston up), it is "putting in effort" and losing energy, so the value is negative.

EXAMPLE 1

Scenario: A system undergoes a process where it absorbs 490 J of heat and does 214 J of work on the surroundings. Calculate $\Delta E$ and $\Delta H$.

Given:
  • Heat ($q$) = $+490 \text{ J}$ (absorbed/positive)
  • Work ($w$) = $-214 \text{ J}$ (done by system/negative)
Calculations:

$\Delta E = q + w$

$\Delta E = 490 \text{ J} + (-214 \text{ J}) = 276 \text{ J}$

$\Delta H = q = 490 \text{ J}$ or $0.49 \text{ kJ}$

Result: The change in internal energy is $+276 \text{ J}$.

SUMMARY

The total energy change of a system is the sum of heat exchanged and work performed. It is critical to ensure units match (converting J to kJ if necessary) before adding $q$ and $w$. While $\Delta E$ accounts for both heat and work, $\Delta H$ specifically focuses on the heat flow at constant pressure. We will spend the rest of the chapter calculating the Enthalpy of Reaction.
