CHM-151: Flame Test and Beer's Law Lab Report

Section 0: Lab Theory & Background

Beer-Lambert Law:

The Beer-Lambert Law ($A = \varepsilon bc$) describes the direct, linear relationship between the absorbance of light and the concentration of a sample. Here, $A$ is absorbance, $\varepsilon$ is the molar absorptivity constant, $b$ is the path length of the cuvette, and $c$ is the concentration.

Wavelength Selection ($\lambda_{max}$):

In this lab, we used a wavelength of $635\text{ nm}$. Because the $CuSO_4$ solution appears blue, it transmits blue light and strongly absorbs its complementary color (orange/red). The $635\text{ nm}$ wavelength falls in this red/orange region, representing the maximum absorbance ($\lambda_{max}$) for our solution.

Visualizing Absorption:

Following color wheel logic, a solution appears a certain color because it absorbs light of its complementary color. By strongly absorbing orange light at $635\text{ nm}$, the solution transmits the remaining light, making it appear blue to our eyes.

Calibration Curve Analysis:

The Equation of the Line ($y = mx + b$): In our plot, the y-axis represents the measured Absorbance, and the x-axis represents the Concentration (M). This line equation allows us to determine the concentration of any unknown sample by plugging in its measured absorbance as $y$ and solving for $x$.

The $R^2$ Value (Coefficient of Determination): The $R^2$ value measures how closely our data points follow the linear trendline. A value close to $1.000$ indicates a strong, highly linear relationship, which reflects excellent precision in our lab technique during the dilution series (careful pipetting and mixing). A lower $R^2$ value would indicate greater variance and experimental error.

Data Analysis Guide: Using Excel
  1. Highlight data: Select your Concentration ($x$) and Absorbance ($y$) columns.
  2. Insert Scatter Plot: Go to the Insert tab and choose a Scatter plot (markers only).
  3. Add Trendline: Right-click on any data point on the graph and select "Add Trendline".
  4. Display Equation: Check the box for "Display Equation on chart".

Once you have your line equation ($y = mx + b$), you can determine the unknown concentration ($x$) by plugging in the measured absorbance ($A_{unknown}$) as $y$:

$$x = \frac{A_{unknown} - b}{m}$$
Sample Calculation for Unknown:

If an unknown has an absorbance of $0.350$ and your calibration line is $y = 45.2x + 0.012$, you can substitute the absorbance for $y$ and solve for the concentration ($x$):

$$x = \frac{0.350 - 0.012}{45.2} = 0.00748\text{ M}$$

Instrument Technique: The Spectronic 20

Best Practices:
  • Always 'Zero' the instrument with a DI Water blank.
  • Wipe cuvettes with Kimwipes to remove fingerprints (oil absorbs light!).
  • Ensure the clear side of the cuvette faces the light path.
  • Allow the lamp to warm up for 15 minutes before taking readings.

Post-Lab Questions

1. Why does each element result in a different color when placed into the flame?

The heat of the flame excites electrons to higher energy levels. When these electrons relax back to their ground state, they release energy in the form of photons. Because each element has a unique electron configuration, the energy gaps between shells are different, resulting in specific wavelengths (colors) of light unique to that element.

2. What is the wavelength of light emitted when an electron relaxes from $n=4$ to $n=2$ in a hydrogen atom? What color would you see?

Using the Rydberg formula:

$$\frac{1}{\lambda} = 1.097 \times 10^7\text{ m}^{-1} \left( \frac{1}{2^2} - \frac{1}{4^2} \right)$$ $$\frac{1}{\lambda} = 1.097 \times 10^7 \left( 0.25 - 0.0625 \right) = 2,056,875\text{ m}^{-1}$$ $$\lambda = 4.86 \times 10^{-7}\text{ m} = \mathbf{486\text{ nm}}$$

Observed Color: Blue-Green

4. Discussion of Experimental Errors
  • (a) Filling solution below the etched line: If the volumetric flask is underfilled, the total volume ($V_2$) is smaller than $25.00\text{ mL}$. This makes the actual concentration of the standard higher than calculated. This would result in a standard curve that overestimates the concentration of the unknowns.
  • (b) Not rinsing the cuvette between solutions: If traces of a higher concentration solution remain in the cuvette, the absorbance reading for the next solution will be artificially high. This shifts the data points and decreases the accuracy ($R^2$) of the standard curve.
  • (c) Not rinsing the stock solution out of the graduated cylinder: If residual stock solution is left behind, the actual amount of moles transferred to the volumetric flask is lower than intended. This makes the actual concentration lower than the calculated concentration.
