Chapter 8: Chemical Bonding I: Basic Concepts

BIG IDEA: Covers the mechanics of ionic and covalent bonding, the Born-Haber cycle, Lewis structures, formal charge, resonance, and bond enthalpies.

Chapter Summary: Key Equations & Definitions

  • Ionic Bond: Electrostatic attraction between cations (positive) and anions (negative). Usually Metal + Nonmetal.
  • Covalent Bond: Electrons are shared between two atoms. Usually Nonmetal + Nonmetal.
Lattice Energy ($E$): $$E = k \frac{Q_1 Q_2}{r}$$

Context: Lattice energy measures the strength of an ionic bond.
Higher charges ($Q$) = Stronger attraction (More negative energy).
Smaller distance ($r$) = Stronger attraction.

8.1 Lewis Dot Symbols

Lewis dot symbols represent the valence electrons of an atom as dots surrounding the element symbol.

The Rule: Place dots on the four sides (top, bottom, left, right) one by one. Do not pair them up until each side has at least one dot.

Example: Nitrogen (Group 15)

8.4 Electronegativity (EN)

Definition: The ability of an atom in a chemical bond to attract shared electrons to itself.

Trend: Increases Up and to the Right.

Deep Dive: Fluorine ($F$) is the "King of Electronegativity" (Value = 4.0). Francium ($Fr$) is the lowest.

The difference in electronegativity ($\Delta EN$) determines the bond type:

8.5 Drawing Lewis Structures (The 5-Step Process)

  1. Count Total Valence Electrons: Sum the valence electrons from all atoms. (Add electrons for anions, subtract for cations).
  2. Draw Skeletal Structure: Place the least electronegative atom in the center (Hydrogen is never central).
  3. Connect Atoms: Use single bonds to connect outer atoms to the central atom. (Subtract 2 electrons from your total for each bond).
  4. Fill Octets of Outer Atoms: Place remaining electrons as lone pairs on the outer atoms first to satisfy the Octet Rule (8 electrons).
  5. Place Remaining Electrons on Center: If any electrons are left, place them on the central atom. (If the center still lacks an octet, convert lone pairs from outer atoms into double/triple bonds).

8.7 Resonance Structures

Definition: Used when a single Lewis structure cannot accurately represent a molecule.

Notation: Use a double-headed arrow ($\leftrightarrow$) between structures.

Key Concept: The actual molecule is an 'average' of all resonance forms, not a back-and-forth flip.

Example: Ozone ($O_3$) or Carbonate ($CO_3^{2-}$).

8.8 Formal Charge (Choosing the Best Structure)

Definition: The electrical charge an atom would have if all electrons in bonds were shared equally.

Equation: $$FC = [Total Valence e^-] - [Nonbonding e^-] - \frac{1}{2} [Bonding e^-]$$
  • Rule 1: The sum of formal charges must equal the overall charge of the molecule/ion.
  • Rule 2: Structures with formal charges closest to zero are preferred.
  • Rule 3: Negative formal charges should reside on the most electronegative atoms.

8.9 Exceptions to the Octet Rule

  • Group 1: Incomplete Octets (e.g., $Be, B$). Boron is happy with only 6 electrons ($BF_3$).
  • Group 2: Odd Number of Electrons (e.g., $NO$). These are called 'Free Radicals'.
  • Group 3: Expanded Octets. Elements in Period 3 and below (like $P, S, Xe$) can have more than 8 electrons because they have empty d-orbitals.
Pro-Tip: Always fill outer octets first; the center atom takes the overflow.

8.10 Bond Enthalpy

Definition: The enthalpy change required to break a particular bond in 1 mole of gaseous molecules.

Key Rule: Bond breaking is ENDOTHERMIC (+) (requires energy), and bond forming is EXOTHERMIC (-) (releases energy).

Equation for Enthalpy of Reaction: $$\Delta H^\circ = \sum BE(\text{reactants}) - \sum BE(\text{products})$$

Context: To use this, you MUST draw the Lewis structures first to count how many of each bond type (single, double, triple) are being broken or formed.

Table 8.5: Average Bond Lengths of Some Common Bonds

Bond Type Length Strength / Enthalpy

Concept Box: Think of it like rubber bands. Adding more bands (bonds) pulls the atoms closer together (shorter length) and makes them much harder to snap apart (higher strength).

8.2 The Born-Haber Cycle: 5 Steps to Ionic Stability

Context: We use this cycle to calculate Lattice Energy ($\Delta H_{lattice}$) because it cannot be measured directly. It follows Hess's Law.

The 5 Steps (using $LiF$ as an example):

  1. Sublimation: Solid metal becomes gas ($\Delta H_{sub}$).
  2. Dissociation: Diatomic nonmetal gas becomes atoms ($\Delta H_{diss}$).
  3. Ionization: Metal gas becomes a cation ($\Delta H_{IE}$).
  4. Electron Affinity: Nonmetal gas becomes an anion ($\Delta H_{EA}$).
  5. Lattice Formation: Gaseous ions form the solid lattice ($\Delta H_{lattice}$). (The Goal)
