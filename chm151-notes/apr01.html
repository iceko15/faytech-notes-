Chemistry Formal Lab Report Architecture

BIG IDEA

The objective is to produce a formal lab report modeled after professional scientific communication, specifically targeting the standard of a peer-reviewed chemistry journal. The report must be technically precise, written in a specific scientific voice, and formatted according to American Chemical Society (ACS) guidelines.

Professor's Warning on AI: Do NOT cite journals suggested by AI (like ChatGPT) if you do not have actual access to them. Citing a source you cannot read will result in a zero on the entire paper. Only cite references you have personally accessed and verified.

REQUIRED REPORT SECTIONS (In Appearance Order)

  1. Cover Page: Includes your name, lab partner's name, and the experiment title.
  2. Abstract: A summary of the entire paper (Purpose, Experiment conducted, General Results, and Conclusions).
    Writing Tip: This is written LAST because it summarizes the other sections.
  3. Introduction: Background information, purpose of the experiment, and definitions (e.g., titration, endpoint, equivalence point). Includes real-world applications and the balanced chemical reaction.
  4. Procedure: A paragraph-style narrative of what was done. It is not a list of instructions.
  5. Results: Raw data presentation (tables) and example calculations.
  6. Discussion: Interpretation of results, comparison to known values (e.g., 5.00% acetic acid in vinegar), and error analysis.
  7. Conclusion: A brief (2-3 sentence) summary of the results.
  8. References: A separate page listing sources in ACS format.

TECHNICAL WRITING RULES

  • Voice: Third Person Passive (Avoid "I," "We," "Me," or "My"). Use "A buret was filled..." instead of "I filled the buret."
  • Tense: Past Tense (You are describing an experiment that has already been completed).
  • Tone: Bland and straightforward. Scientific communication lacks "creative writing" or unnecessary descriptors.
  • Equation Formatting: Use a legitimate Equation Editor. Calculations must look like fractions/math, not text strings (e.g., no "7.0 moles / 6.7").

RESULTS & CALCULATIONS GUIDELINES

The results section must be comprehensive, including:

  • Raw Data: Show the numbers collected in-lab (starting volumes, final volumes, total volume used).
  • Example Calculations: Show exactly one example for every calculation type (Total Volume, Molarity of Acetic Acid, Percent Acetic Acid, Percent Error).
    • You only need to show the full calculation for Trial 1.
    • For subsequent trials, state: "The same procedure was followed for Trials 2 and 3."
    • Present the final calculated values for all trials in a summary table.
  • Textual Explanation: Every table and calculation needs a written explanation in paragraph form explaining what the reader is looking at.

ACS CITATION PROTOCOL

The American Chemical Society (ACS) format differs significantly from MLA/APA:

Feature ACS Requirement
In-Text Citations Use superscripted numbers ($^1$, $^2$, $^3$).
Reference Order Listed in the order they appear in the paper, NOT alphabetical.
Number Persistence Once a source is assigned a number, it keeps that number throughout the paper.
Textbook Citations Must include specific page numbers (e.g., pp. 10-14).

ERROR ANALYSIS (DISCUSSION SECTION)

You must compare your experimental percentage of acetic acid to the known value ($5.00\%$).

  • Calculate Percent Error: $$ \text{Percent Error} = \frac{|\text{Experimental} - \text{Theoretical}|}{\text{Theoretical}} \times 100 $$
  • Explain Directionality: If your result is high (e.g., 6%), what specific laboratory error caused the increase? If low (e.g., 4%), what caused the decrease?
  • Suggest improvements to technique or procedure to mitigate these errors in the future.
The "Dr. A" Workflow (Optimal Writing Order):
1. Procedure (Easiest to write)
2. Results & Calculations
3. Discussion & Conclusion
4. Introduction & References (Do these together)
5. Abstract (Do last)

GRADING & TIMELINE

  • Draft Day: Bring a printed copy to lab. Complete a peer exchange and provide feedback. (Completion grade).
  • Peer Review Copy: Graded as a regular lab report. You will receive feedback from the professor.
  • Final Report: Worth 5% of the overall course grade.
  • Formatting Specs: Strictly follow the rubric for font, font size, and spacing. Journals are highly picky.
