LECTURE NOTES: Chemical Reaction Classifications (Jan 28)

Section 1: BIG IDEA

Understanding the categorization of chemical reactions allows chemists to predict products and balance equations accurately. This lab focuses on identifying reactions through observable evidence—such as color changes, gas production, and heat—and translating word descriptions into balanced symbolic equations.

Instructor Emphasis: Always identify the reactants' nature (e.g., hydrocarbons, acids, bases) to determine the reaction type before balancing.

Section 2: KEY TERMS / CONCEPTS

Reaction Type Description / Identifying Features
Combustion A hydrocarbon fuel reacts with Oxygen ($O_2$) to produce Carbon Dioxide ($CO_2$) and Water ($H_2O$).
Acid-Base Neutralization An acid reacts with a base (often containing OH) to produce Water ($H_2O$) and a Salt (ionic compound).
Decomposition A single reactant breaks down into two or more products, often requiring heat (e.g., heating hydrates).
Combination (Synthesis) Two or more substances combine to form a single new compound (e.g., burning Magnesium).
Single Replacement One element replaces another in a compound (e.g., Magnesium reacting with an acid).
Double Replacement The ions of two compounds exchange places in an aqueous solution to form two new compounds.

Section 3: STEPS / PROCESS

  1. Observe: Note physical changes such as bubbling (gas), precipitation (solid), or temperature changes.
  2. Identify Reactants: Determine if you have a hydrocarbon, an acid/base pair, or a single metal element.
  3. Translate: Convert the names of chemicals into chemical symbols and formulas (e.g., Magnesium Oxide becomes $MgO$).
  4. Balance: Adjust coefficients to ensure the number of atoms for each element is equal on both sides of the equation.

Section 4: RULES / IMPORTANT NOTES

  • Hydrocarbon Rule: Any fuel made of only Hydrogen and Carbon (like methane or octane) is a hydrocarbon.
  • Definition of a Salt: In chemistry, a "salt" refers to any ionic compound formed from an acid-base reaction, not just table salt ($NaCl$).
  • Incomplete Combustion: Burning fuels in enclosed spaces can produce Carbon Monoxide ($CO$) due to limited oxygen.
  • Acid Identification: Look for compounds that produce $H^+$ ions; Bases often contain Hydroxide ($OH$) groups.
