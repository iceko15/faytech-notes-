Chapter 7: Electron Configuration & Periodic Table

Chapter Summary: Key Equations

Effective Nuclear Charge: $$Z_{eff} = Z - \sigma$$
  • $Z$ = Atomic Number (number of protons)
  • $\sigma$ = Shielding Constant (screening by core electrons)

7.3 Effective Nuclear Charge ($Z_{eff}$)

$Z_{eff}$ is the actual positive charge felt by an electron, accounting for the shielding effect of other electrons.

Context: Shielding happens because inner (core) electrons repel the outer (valence) electrons, protecting them from the full pull of the nucleus.

Deep Dive: As you move right across a period, $Z_{eff}$ increases because you add protons but the number of core shielding electrons stays the same.

7.4 Atomic Radius

Rule: Atomic radius decreases from Left-to-Right and increases from Top-to-Bottom.

Why: Increasing $Z_{eff}$ across a period pulls the electrons in tighter, reducing the radius.

Deep Dive: Listen for 'Covalent Radius' (nonmetals) vs 'Metallic Radius' (metals).

7.4 Ionization Energy (IE)

Definition: The energy required to remove an electron from a gaseous atom or ion.

Rule: Ionization energy increases from Left-to-Right and decreases from Top-to-Bottom.

Look Out: Core electrons are MUCH harder to remove than valence electrons.
Pitfall: Group 13 has a lower IE than Group 2 because of the subshell shift (s to p). Removing the single p-electron is easier than breaking the filled s-subshell.
Exception (Group 15 vs 16): Nitrogen (Group 15) has a higher IE than Oxygen (Group 16).
Reason: Oxygen has its first paired electron in the p-subshell ($2p^4$). The repulsion between those two electrons makes it slightly easier to remove one, despite Oxygen having more protons.

7.5 Electron Affinity (EA)

Definition: The energy change that occurs when an electron is added to a gaseous atom.

Rule: Generally increases (becomes more negative) from Left-to-Right.

Context: Nonmetals want electrons more than metals do because they are closer to achieving a full octet.

Deep Dive: Look out for Noble Gases—they have a positive (unfavorable) EA because you'd have to start a brand new energy level to add an electron.

7.6 Ionic Radius (Size of Ions)

  • Cations (+): Always SMALLER than their parent atom. (Losing electrons means less repulsion and sometimes losing an entire energy level).
  • Anions (-): Always LARGER than their parent atom. (Adding electrons increases repulsion, pushing them further apart).
Visual Rule: $$Li^+ < Li \quad | \quad F^- > F$$

7.6 The Isoelectronic Series

Definition: A group of ions/atoms that have the same number of electrons (e.g., $O^{2-}, F^-, Ne, Na^+, Mg^{2+}$ all have 10 electrons).

The Logic: Since the electron count is the same, the size is determined ONLY by the number of protons ($Z$).

Rule: The more protons in the nucleus, the smaller the ion. $$Mg^{2+} \text{ (Smallest)} < Na^+ < Ne < F^- < O^{2-} \text{ (Largest)}$$

7.4 Metallic Character

Definition: The level of reactivity of a metal.

Rule: Increases Right-to-Left and Top-to-Bottom (opposite of Ionization Energy).

Context: The easier it is to remove an electron (low IE), the more metallic the element is.

Deep Dive: This is why Cesium ($Cs$) is one of the most reactive metals - it's at the bottom left.

The "Up and Right" Rule: Elements in the top-right (Helium) have the highest IE and smallest Radius. Elements in the bottom-left (Francium) have the largest Radius and lowest IE.

The Transition Metal Rule: 'First In, First Out'

Context: When d-block elements form cations, electrons are removed from the $ns$ orbital BEFORE the $(n-1)d$ orbital.

Why: Once the d-orbital starts filling, the s-orbital actually becomes higher in energy, making those electrons easier to remove.

Example:
  • Iron ($Fe$): $[Ar] 4s^2 3d^6$
  • Iron (II) ion ($Fe^{2+}$): $[Ar] 3d^6$ (The $4s$ electrons are gone first)
REMEMBER FOR EXAMS: When transition metals like Fe form ions, the 4s electrons are lost FIRST. Fe is $[Ar] 4s^2 3d^6$, but $Fe^{2+}$ is $[Ar] 3d^6$.

7.4 Successive Ionization Energies (The "Core" Cliff)

Ionization energy increases for each subsequent electron removed ($IE_1 < IE_2 < IE_3$).

Magnesium ($Mg$) Data

Ionization Step Energy ($kJ/mol$) Electron Type
$IE_1$ 738 Removing 1st valence
$IE_2$ 1451 Removing 2nd valence
$IE_3$ 7733 THE CLIFF - Removing a core electron

Context: The massive jump in energy (the 'cliff') tells you exactly how many valence electrons an atom has. Using Equation 7.1 ($Z_{eff} = Z - \sigma$), we see that core electrons feel a much higher $Z_{eff}$ because there are fewer shielding electrons ($\sigma$) between them and the nucleus.

7.7 Group Properties (Chemical Trends)

  • Group 1 (Alkali Metals): Most reactive metals; reactivity increases going down.
  • Group 17 (Halogens): Most reactive nonmetals; reactivity increases going up.
  • Group 18 (Noble Gases): Extremely stable due to completely filled subshells ($ns^2 np^6$).
  • Oxides Trend: Metallic oxides are Basic; Nonmetallic oxides are Acidic. (Amphoteric oxides like $Al_2O_3$ can be both).

Chapter 7 Knowledge Check

  1. Which is larger: $Cl$ or $Cl^-$?
  2. Why does Atomic Radius decrease as you move left-to-right across a period?
  3. Rank in order of increasing size: $N^{3-}, F^-, Mg^{2+}$.
Click for Answer Key
  • 1. $Cl^-$ (Anions are always larger).
  • 2. Increasing $Z_{eff}$ pulls electrons closer.
  • 3. $Mg^{2+} < F^- < N^{3-}$ (The Isoelectronic Rule: more protons = smaller size).
