BIG IDEA

Chapter 4 focuses on Aqueous Solutions—mixtures where water is the solvent. We explore how ionic compounds dissociate into electrolytes, how to predict solubility using specific rules, and how to represent chemical changes through Molecular, Complete Ionic, and Net Ionic equations.

KEY TERMS: SOLUTION CHEMISTRY

Term Definition
Solution A homogeneous mixture containing a solvent and a solute.
Solvent The larger part of the solution. In this course, the solvent is always Water.
Solute The smaller part of the solution that is dissolved (e.g., salt, sugar).
Aqueous Solution A specific type of solution where the solvent is water. Symbol: $(aq)$.
Dissociation The process where ionic compounds split into their constituent ions when dissolved.
Solvation / Hydration Solvation is when solute particles are surrounded by solvent. Hydration is the specific term when that solvent is water.

ELECTROLYTES

Electricity in solution is the movement of charged particles (ions). Pure water does not conduct electricity; the ions dissolved in it do.

1. Strong Electrolytes

  • Behavior: Fully dissociate (100% split into ions).
  • Observation: Light bulb glows brightly.
  • Examples: Soluble Ionic Compounds (Salts), Strong Acids (e.g., $HCl$), Strong Bases.
  • Equation: $NaCl(s) \rightarrow Na^+(aq) + Cl^-(aq)$

2. Weak Electrolytes

  • Behavior: Partially dissociate (only a small fraction splits into ions).
  • Observation: Light bulb glows dimly.
  • Examples: Weak Acids (e.g., $HF$, Acetic Acid), Weak Bases.

3. Non-Electrolytes

  • Behavior: Dissolve but do not dissociate (remain as whole molecules).
  • Observation: Light bulb does not turn on.
  • Examples: Molecular compounds like Sugar ($C_{12}H_{22}O_{11}$), Alcohols, Pure/Distilled Water.
Real World Note: "Electrolytes" in Gatorade are just salts (Sodium, Potassium, Magnesium). Also, don't drop electronics in water—it's not the water that shocks you, it's the ions dissolved in it. Distilled (deionized) water is an insulator.

SOLUBILITY RULES

Not all ionic compounds dissolve in water. We use these rules to predict if a compound is Soluble (Aqueous) or Insoluble (Solid/Precipitate).

Always Soluble (No Exceptions)

  • Group 1 Cations: $Li^+, Na^+, K^+, Rb^+, Cs^+$.
  • Ammonium: $NH_4^+$.
  • Nitrate: $NO_3^-$.
  • Acetate: $C_2H_3O_2^-$ (and Chlorate $ClO_3^-$).

Usually Soluble (With Exceptions)

  • Halides ($Cl^-, Br^-, I^-$): Soluble EXCEPT when paired with Silver ($Ag^+$), Mercury ($Hg_2^{2+}$), or Lead ($Pb^{2+}$).
  • Sulfates ($SO_4^{2-}$): Soluble EXCEPT when paired with $Ag^+, Hg_2^{2+}, Pb^{2+}$, Calcium ($Ca^{2+}$), Strontium ($Sr^{2+}$), or Barium ($Ba^{2+}$).

Insoluble (Unless Rule 1 Applies)

  • Carbonates ($CO_3^{2-}$), Hydroxides ($OH^-$), Phosphates ($PO_4^{3-}$), etc. are generally insoluble unless paired with Group 1 or Ammonium.

REACTION TYPES: PRECIPITATION

A Double Replacement (or Metathesis) reaction occurs when two aqueous solutions are mixed, and cations swap partners. If a product is insoluble, a solid forms (Precipitate).

The Three Equations

  1. Molecular Equation: Shows neutral formulas for all reactants and products.
  2. Complete Ionic Equation: Breaks all strong electrolytes (aqueous) into ions. Solids remain intact.
  3. Net Ionic Equation: Removes Spectator Ions (ions that do not change) to show only the reaction forming the solid.

Example 1: Magnesium Nitrate + Sodium Hydroxide

1. Predict Products & Balance (Molecular):

$$Mg(NO_3)_2(aq) + 2NaOH(aq) \rightarrow Mg(OH)_2(s) + 2NaNO_3(aq)$$

Logic: $Mg(OH)_2$ is insoluble (Hydroxides are insoluble except Group 1). $NaNO_3$ is soluble (Group 1 + Nitrate).

2. Complete Ionic:

$$Mg^{2+}(aq) + 2NO_3^-(aq) + 2Na^+(aq) + 2OH^-(aq) \rightarrow Mg(OH)_2(s) + 2Na^+(aq) + 2NO_3^-(aq)$$

3. Identify Spectators:

$2Na^+$ and $2NO_3^-$ appear unchanged on both sides.

4. Net Ionic:

$$Mg^{2+}(aq) + 2OH^-(aq) \rightarrow Mg(OH)_2(s)$$

Example 2: Iron(III) Nitrate + Sodium Hydroxide

Reactants: $Fe(NO_3)_3(aq) + NaOH(aq)$

Swap Partners: Iron goes with Hydroxide ($Fe^{3+} + OH^- \rightarrow Fe(OH)_3$). Sodium goes with Nitrate ($Na^+ + NO_3^- \rightarrow NaNO_3$).

Solubility Check: $Fe(OH)_3$ is a Solid (Insoluble). $NaNO_3$ is Aqueous.

Balanced Molecular:

$$Fe(NO_3)_3(aq) + 3NaOH(aq) \rightarrow Fe(OH)_3(s) + 3NaNO_3(aq)$$

Net Ionic Strategy: Ignore the aqueous product ($NaNO_3$) and its ions. Focus on the solid.

$$Fe^{3+}(aq) + 3OH^-(aq) \rightarrow Fe(OH)_3(s)$$
Balancing Tip: If polyatomic ions (like Nitrate $NO_3$) appear on both sides, count them as a single unit. Don't count individual Nitrogens and Oxygens. It saves massive amounts of time.

Example 3: Lithium Hydroxide + Copper(II) Nitrate

Question: Which represents the Net Ionic Equation?

Analysis:

  • Reactants: $LiOH$ (Soluble/Group 1) and $Cu(NO_3)_2$ (Soluble/Nitrate).
  • Possible Products: $LiNO_3$ (Soluble) and $Cu(OH)_2$ (Insoluble/Solid).
  • The reaction produces solid Copper(II) Hydroxide.

Net Ionic Equation:

$$Cu^{2+}(aq) + 2OH^-(aq) \rightarrow Cu(OH)_2(s)$$

ACIDS AND BASES (INTRO)

Definitions

Type Arrhenius Definition Bronsted-Lowry Definition
Acid Produces $H^+$ in water. Proton ($H^+$) Donor.
Base Produces $OH^-$ in water. Proton ($H^+$) Acceptor.

Note: A Hydrogen ion ($H^+$) is simply a proton (it lost its only electron). Thus, acids are "Proton Donors."

Amphoteric: A substance that can act as both an acid and a base (e.g., Water).

Strong Acids (Memorize These 7)

These are strong electrolytes and fully dissociate.

  1. Hydrochloric Acid ($HCl$)
  2. Hydrobromic Acid ($HBr$)
  3. Hydroiodic Acid ($HI$)
  4. Nitric Acid ($HNO_3$)
  5. Sulfuric Acid ($H_2SO_4$)
  6. Chloric Acid ($HClO_3$)
  7. Perchloric Acid ($HClO_4$)

SUMMARY

We classify solutes based on their ability to conduct electricity (electrolytes). Using solubility rules, we can predict the outcome of double replacement reactions. If all ions remain aqueous (spectators), No Reaction has occurred. If a solid forms, we represent the chemical change using a Net Ionic Equation.
