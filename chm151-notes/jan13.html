Intro to Chemistry: Matter & Measurements

Instructor: Dr. Able (Tim)
Course: CHM-151 (General Chemistry I)

BIG IDEA

Chemistry is the study of matter—defined as anything that has mass and takes up space—including its properties, composition, and the changes it undergoes. To understand matter, we must categorize it by its state and composition while using the standardized Metric System (SI) for all quantitative observations.

KEY TERMS

Term Definition
Matter Anything that possesses mass and occupies physical space.
Pure Substance Matter with a fixed composition (Elements or Compounds).
Mixture A physical combination of two or more substances that are not chemically bonded.
Homogeneous A mixture that is uniform throughout (also called a solution).
Heterogeneous A mixture with distinct, visible phases or components.
Compound A substance composed of two or more different elements chemically bonded.

STATES OF MATTER

  • Solid: Particles are locked in a rigid structure; they vibrate but do not move from their positions.
  • Liquid: Particles are close together but can slide/flow past one another.
  • Gas: Particles are far apart, moving at high velocities with minimal interaction.

THE SI MEASUREMENT SYSTEM

Chemistry relies on the metric system. Base units include:

  • Length: Meter (\(m\))
  • Mass: Kilogram (\(kg\)) / Gram (\(g\))
  • Time: Second (\(s\))
  • Temperature: Kelvin (\(K\)) or Celsius (\(^\circ C\))
  • Amount: Mole (\(mol\))

TEMPERATURE CONVERSIONS

Kelvin is the "absolute" scale. There are no negative values in Kelvin. Absolute zero is \(0\text{ K}\).

Formula: \[K = ^\circ C + 273.15\] Formula: \[^\circ F = (1.8 \times ^\circ C) + 32\]

METRIC PREFIXES (Kilo to Nano)

Prefix Symbol Meaning Power of 10
Kilo k 1,000 (Thousand) \(10^3\)
Deci d 0.1 (Tenth) \(10^{-1}\)
Centi c 0.01 (Hundredth) \(10^{-2}\)
Milli m 0.001 (Thousandth) \(10^{-3}\)
Micro \(\mu\) 0.000001 (Millionth) \(10^{-6}\)
Nano n 0.000000001 (Billionth) \(10^{-9}\)

SEPARATION OF MIXTURES

  1. Filtration: Separates solids from liquids (or gases) using a porous barrier.
  2. Distillation: Separates liquids based on differences in boiling points.
  3. Chromatography: Separates components based on their ability to travel across a surface.

EXAMPLE DEMONSTRATIONS

1. Element vs. Compound:
\(O_2\) is a molecule (two atoms bonded) but an element (only one type of atom).
\(H_2O\) is a molecule and a compound (two different elements bonded).
2. Temperature Conversion:
If a solution is \(25^\circ C\), what is the temperature in Kelvin?
\(25 + 273.15 = 298.15\text{ K}\)
3. Metric Scale:
\(1\text{ kilogram (kg)} = 1,000\text{ grams (g)}\)
\(1\text{ gram (g)} = 1,000\text{ milligrams (mg)}\)

PRACTICE PROBLEMS

  • Identify as Homogeneous or Heterogeneous: Saltwater. (Homogeneous/Solution)
  • True or False: Every compound is a molecule. (True)
  • Convert \(100^\circ C\) to Kelvin. (373.15 K)
  • Is the symbol for Cobalt "CO" or "Co"? (Co - second letter must be lowercase)

SUMMARY

Success in CHM 151 requires mastering the language of chemistry (symbols and definitions) and the math of the metric system. Matter is classified by how its particles are arranged and whether its composition is pure or mixed. Always ensure capitalization is correct in symbols, as \(Co\) and \(CO\) represent entirely different substances.
