Calorimetry Lab Post Lab Questions

1. What would be the effect on the calculated value of the specific heat of the metal if the following errors occurred? Would the calculated value increase, decrease, or stay the same? Explain your reasoning.

  • a. Some hot water clung to outside of the metal object and was added to the calorimeter.
    Increase. The extra hot water adds additional thermal energy to the calorimeter that did not originate from the metal itself. [cite: 125] This causes the water to reach a higher final temperature, leading to a larger calculated heat gain (q) and an artificially high specific heat.
  • b. The Styrofoam cups were not adequate as insulators and some of the heat was lost to the surroundings.
    Decrease. If heat escapes to the environment, the water in the calorimeter will not reach its true maximum temperature. [cite: 126] This results in a smaller measured ΔT for the water, which makes the calculated specific heat (Cs) appear lower than it actually is.

2. What would be the effect on the calculated value of the heat of formation of MgO if the following errors occurred. Would the calculated value increase, decrease, or stay the same? Explain your reasoning.

  • a. Unknown to the student, about 15% of the Mg metal was spilled when adding it to the HCl solution.
    Increase (Less Negative). The heat produced (q) will be lower because less magnesium actually reacted. However, the calculation divides this smaller heat by the original, larger recorded mass (moles). Dividing a smaller numerator by a larger denominator results in a calculated ΔH with a smaller magnitude (e.g., -400 kJ/mol instead of -600 kJ/mol), which is an increase in value.
  • b. Instead of using the 1.3-1.5 g of magnesium oxide as called for in the experiment, a student weighed out and recorded 2.0 g of MgO.
    Stay the same. Enthalpy of formation is an intensive property (kJ/mol). [cite: 130] As long as the actual mass used (2.0 g) is correctly recorded and used to calculate the moles, the ratio of heat produced to moles reacted remains consistent.

3. The specific heat capacity of silver is 0.24 J/°C•g. If it takes 1.25 kJ of energy to heat a sample of pure silver from 12.0 °C to 15.2 °C, calculate the mass of the sample of silver.

\( q = 1.25 \text{ kJ} = 1250 \text{ J} \) [cite: 131]
\( \Delta T = 15.2^\circ\text{C} - 12.0^\circ\text{C} = 3.2^\circ\text{C} \)
\( m = \frac{q}{C \cdot \Delta T} = \frac{1250 \text{ J}}{0.24 \frac{\text{J}}{\text{g}\cdot^\circ\text{C}} \cdot 3.2^\circ\text{C}} = \mathbf{1627.6 \text{ g}} \)

4. In a coffee-cup calorimeter, 1.60 g of NH4NO3 is dissolved in 75.0 g of water initially at 25.00 °C. The final temperature of the solution is 23.34 °C. Assume that no heat is lost to the surroundings and that the heat capacity of the solution is 4.184 J/°C•g. Calculate the enthalpy change for the dissolution of NH4NO3 in units of kJ/mol.

\( m_{soln} = 75.0 \text{ g} + 1.60 \text{ g} = 76.6 \text{ g} \) [cite: 132]
\( \Delta T = 23.34^\circ\text{C} - 25.00^\circ\text{C} = -1.66^\circ\text{C} \)
\( q_{soln} = 76.6 \text{ g} \cdot 4.184 \frac{\text{J}}{\text{g}\cdot^\circ\text{C}} \cdot (-1.66^\circ\text{C}) = -532.0 \text{ J} \)
\( q_{rxn} = -q_{soln} = +532.0 \text{ J} = 0.532 \text{ kJ} \)

Moles NH4NO3 = \( \frac{1.60 \text{ g}}{80.04 \text{ g/mol}} = 0.01999 \text{ mol} \)
\( \Delta H = \frac{0.532 \text{ kJ}}{0.01999 \text{ mol}} = \mathbf{+26.6 \text{ kJ/mol}} \)
