Report Form Calculations (Part A)

1. Calculate $\Delta H$ (kJ/mol) for Reaction 1 (Mg + HCl).

Step A: Heat of Solution ($q_{soln}$)

$$m_{HCl} = 99.1440\text{ g}$$ $$q_{soln} = m \cdot C \cdot \Delta T = (99.1440\text{ g})(4.184\text{ J/g}\cdot^\circ\text{C})(19.7^\circ\text{C}) = 8171.9\text{ J} = 8.1719\text{ kJ}$$

Step B: Heat of Reaction ($q_{rxn}$)

$$q_{rxn} = -q_{soln} = -8.1719\text{ kJ}$$

Step C: Molar Enthalpy

$$n_{Mg} = \frac{0.4532\text{ g}}{24.31\text{ g/mol}} = 0.01864\text{ mol}$$ $$\Delta H_1 = \frac{-8.1719\text{ kJ}}{0.01864\text{ mol}} = \mathbf{-438.3\text{ kJ/mol}}$$
2. Calculate $\Delta H$ (kJ/mol) for Reaction 2 (MgO + HCl).

Step A: Heat of Solution ($q_{soln}$)

$$m_{HCl} = 100.0295\text{ g}$$ $$q_{soln} = (100.0295\text{ g})(4.184\text{ J/g}\cdot^\circ\text{C})(11.7^\circ\text{C}) = 4896.7\text{ J} = 4.8967\text{ kJ}$$

Step B: Heat of Reaction ($q_{rxn}$)

$$q_{rxn} = -4.8967\text{ kJ}$$

Step C: Molar Enthalpy

$$n_{MgO} = \frac{1.3524\text{ g}}{40.30\text{ g/mol}} = 0.03356\text{ mol}$$ $$\Delta H_2 = \frac{-4.8967\text{ kJ}}{0.03356\text{ mol}} = \mathbf{-145.9\text{ kJ/mol}}$$
3. Determine $\Delta H_f$ for MgO using Hess's Law.

Target: $Mg(s) + \frac{1}{2}O_2(g) \rightarrow MgO(s)$

Logic for Reaction 2: In our experiment (Reaction 2), MgO was a reactant ($MgO + HCl \rightarrow ...$). However, in the target formation equation, MgO is a product. Therefore, we must flip Reaction 2 and reverse the sign of its $\Delta H$.

  • Rxn 1: $Mg + 2HCl \rightarrow MgCl_2 + H_2$ ($\Delta H = -438.3\text{ kJ}$)
  • Rxn 2 (Flipped): $MgCl_2 + H_2O \rightarrow MgO + 2HCl$ ($\Delta H = +145.9\text{ kJ}$)
  • Rxn 3 (Formation of Water): $H_2 + \frac{1}{2}O_2 \rightarrow H_2O$ ($\Delta H = -285.8\text{ kJ}$)
$$\Delta H_{target} = (-438.3) + (145.9) + (-285.8) = -578.2\text{ kJ/mol}$$ $$\mathbf{\Delta H_f = -578.2\text{ kJ/mol}}$$
4. Calculate Percent Error. $$\% \text{ Error} = \left| \frac{\text{Experimental} - \text{Accepted}}{\text{Accepted}} \right| \times 100$$ $$\% \text{ Error} = \left| \frac{-578.2 - (-601.8)}{-601.8} \right| \times 100 = \left| \frac{23.6}{-601.8} \right| \times 100 = \mathbf{3.92\%}$$

Report Form Calculations (Part B)

5. Calculate heat gained by water ($q_{water}$). $$q_{water} = m_{water} \cdot C_{water} \cdot \Delta T_{water}$$ $$q_{water} = (101.3012\text{ g})(4.184\text{ J/g}\cdot^\circ\text{C})(4.1^\circ\text{C}) = \mathbf{1737.9\text{ J}}$$
6. Calculate Specific Heat ($C_s$) of the metal.

Assumption: $-q_{metal} = q_{water} \Rightarrow q_{metal} = -1737.9\text{ J}$

$$C_{metal} = \frac{q_{metal}}{m_{metal} \cdot \Delta T_{metal}}$$ $$C_{metal} = \frac{-1737.9\text{ J}}{(25.3021\text{ g})(-75.9^\circ\text{C})} = \frac{-1737.9}{-1920.4} = \mathbf{0.905\text{ J/g}\cdot^\circ\text{C}}$$

Percent Error:

$$\% \text{ Error} = \left| \frac{0.905 - 0.900}{0.900} \right| \times 100 = \mathbf{0.56\%}$$

Post-Lab Questions

7. Sources of Error (Specific Heat)
  • (a) Hot water adheres to metal: If hot water from the boiling bath sticks to the metal, it adds extra heat to the calorimeter water. This makes $q_{water}$ artificially high, leading to a higher calculated specific heat ($C_s$).
  • (b) Poor insulation: If heat escapes the calorimeter, $\Delta T_{water}$ will be lower than it should be. This results in a lower $q_{water}$ and a lower calculated specific heat ($C_s$).
8. Sources of Error (Enthalpy of Formation)
  • (a) Spilled Mg: If Mg is lost but recorded as used, the actual moles reacting is lower than the calculation assumes. The observed heat ($q$) will be smaller, resulting in a smaller magnitude for $\Delta H$ (less negative).
  • (b) Recording 2.0g MgO: If you actually used 1.35g but calculated using 2.0g, your mole count ($n$) in the denominator will be artificially large. Dividing the heat by a larger number results in a smaller (decreased) molar enthalpy.
9. Silver Mass Calculation

Problem: Calculate the mass of silver ($C_s = 0.240\text{ J/g}\cdot^\circ\text{C}$) that absorbs $10.0\text{ kJ}$ of heat when its temperature rises by $25.6^\circ\text{C}$.

$$q = 10.0\text{ kJ} = 10000\text{ J}$$ $$m = \frac{q}{C \cdot \Delta T} = \frac{10000\text{ J}}{(0.240\text{ J/g}\cdot^\circ\text{C})(25.6^\circ\text{C})}$$ $$m = \frac{10000}{6.144} = \mathbf{1627.6\text{ g}}$$
10. $NH_4NO_3$ Dissolution Calculation

Problem: Dissolve $1.60\text{ g}$ $NH_4NO_3$ in $75.0\text{ g}$ water. $T_i = 25.00^\circ\text{C}$, $T_f = 23.34^\circ\text{C}$.

$$m_{total} = 1.60\text{ g} + 75.0\text{ g} = 76.6\text{ g}$$ $$\Delta T = 23.34^\circ\text{C} - 25.00^\circ\text{C} = -1.66^\circ\text{C}$$ $$q_{soln} = (76.6\text{ g})(4.184\text{ J/g}\cdot^\circ\text{C})(-1.66^\circ\text{C}) = -532.0\text{ J}$$ $$q_{soln} = -0.532\text{ kJ}$$ $$q_{rxn} = -q_{soln} = +0.532\text{ kJ}$$ $$n = \frac{1.60\text{ g}}{80.05\text{ g/mol}} = 0.01999\text{ mol}$$ $$\Delta H = \frac{+0.532\text{ kJ}}{0.01999\text{ mol}} = \mathbf{+26.6\text{ kJ/mol}}$$
11. Hess's Law Summation

Problem: Calculate $\Delta H$ for $2B + 3H_2 \rightarrow B_2H_6$ given:

  • 1) $2B + \frac{3}{2}O_2 \rightarrow B_2O_3$ ($\Delta H = -1273\text{ kJ}$)
  • 2) $B_2H_6 + 3O_2 \rightarrow B_2O_3 + 3H_2O$ ($\Delta H = -2035\text{ kJ}$)
  • 3) $H_2 + \frac{1}{2}O_2 \rightarrow H_2O$ ($\Delta H = -242\text{ kJ}$)

Strategy:

  • Equation 1: Keep as is ($-1273\text{ kJ}$)
  • Equation 2: Reverse sign ($+2035\text{ kJ}$)
  • Equation 3: Multiply by 3 ($-726\text{ kJ}$)
$$\Delta H_{total} = (-1273) + (2035) + (-726) = \mathbf{+36\text{ kJ}}$$
