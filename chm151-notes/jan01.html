January 1: Stoichiometry, Quantum Numbers, and Bonding

Section 1: BIG IDEA

This session bridges the gap between macroscopic chemical quantities and microscopic atomic behavior. We focus on the precise calculation of molar masses and limiting reactants to predict reaction yields. Furthermore, we explore the quantum mechanical model of the atom and how electron configurations dictate periodic trends like electronegativity and the nature of chemical bonds.

Section 2: KEY TERMS

Term Definition
Molar Mass The mass of one mole of a substance (g/mol), calculated using precise atomic weights from the periodic table.
Limiting Reactant The substance that is totally consumed when the chemical reaction is complete, limiting the amount of product formed.
Aufbau Principle Electrons fill the lowest energy orbitals available before moving to higher levels.
Hund's Rule Electrons will occupy degenerate orbitals (same energy) singly before pairing up to minimize repulsion.
Electronegativity A measure of the tendency of an atom to attract a bonding pair of electrons.

Section 3: STEPS/PROCESS

  1. Balance the Equation: You cannot perform stoichiometry without correct coefficients.
  2. Convert to Moles: Use the molar mass (e.g., Carbon = 12.01 g/mol) to convert grams to moles.
    Given Mass (g) Molar Mass (g/mol)
  3. Apply Mole Ratio: Use coefficients from the balanced equation to find the moles of the desired substance.
  4. Identify Limiting Reactant: Compare the required amount of each reactant to the actual amount available.

Section 4: RULES / IMPORTANT NOTES

  • Isotope Awareness: Always use the average atomic mass (decimal) from the periodic table, not the whole mass number.
  • Quantum Numbers:
    • n: Principal number (energy level/shell).
    • l: Angular momentum (shape). l=0 (s, sphere), l=1 (p, dumbbell).
  • Exceptions: Chromium (Cr) and Copper (Cu) follow unique configurations due to the stability of half-filled or fully-filled d subshells.
  • Trends: Electronegativity increases up and to the right (Fluorine is the most electronegative). Noble gases are generally excluded.

Section 5: EXAMPLE / DEMONSTRATION

1. The "Sandwich" Analogy: If 2 slices of bread + 1 slice of cheese = 1 sandwich. If you have 20 bread and 1 cheese, the cheese is the limiting reactant.
2. Polar Covalent Bonding: In a H-Cl bond, Cl is more electronegative. It "shares" the electrons but pulls them closer to its side, creating a dipole.
3. Hund's Rule: Imagine a bus (subshell). Passengers (electrons) take empty rows (orbitals) first before sitting next to someone else.

Section 6: PRACTICE PROBLEMS

  1. If you have 24.02g of Carbon, how many moles do you have? (2.00 moles)
  2. Which quantum number determines the shape of an orbital? (The angular momentum quantum number, l)
  3. What is the electron configuration exception for Copper (Cu)? ([Ar] 4s1 3d10)

Section 7: SUMMARY

Mastering chemistry requires balancing the "math" of stoichiometry with the "logic" of atomic structure. Always start with a balanced equation and precise molar masses. When moving to the atom, remember that electron behavior (Aufbau, Hund's Rule) and electronegativity trends dictate how atoms will eventually bond (Ionic vs. Covalent).
