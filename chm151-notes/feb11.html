Copper Cycle Lab: Full Analysis

CHEMICAL REACTIONS & OBSERVATIONS

Reaction 1: Dissolution with Nitric Acid

$Cu(s) + 4HNO_3(aq) \rightarrow Cu(NO_3)_2(aq) + 2NO_2(g) + 2H_2O(l)$

  • Reaction Type: Oxidation-Reduction (Redox) / Single Replacement.
  • What is in solution? Copper (II) nitrate, $Cu(NO_3)_2(aq)$.
  • Observations: Vigorous gas production (bubbles); liquid turns green; brown gas emission.

Reaction 2: Precipitation with Sodium Hydroxide

$Cu(NO_3)_2(aq) + 2NaOH(aq) \rightarrow Cu(OH)_2(s) + 2NaNO_3(aq)$

  • Reaction Type: Double Replacement (Precipitation).
  • Side Reaction: $HNO_3(aq) + NaOH(aq) \rightarrow NaNO_3(aq) + H_2O(l)$ (Neutralization).
  • Second Reaction Products: Sodium Nitrate ($NaNO_3$) and Water ($H_2O$).
  • Observations: Solution turns royal blue with thick precipitate.

Reaction 3: Thermal Decomposition

$Cu(OH)_2(s) \rightarrow CuO(s) + H_2O(l)$

  • Reaction Type: Decomposition.
  • What is removed by decanting? Excess soluble sodium nitrate ($NaNO_3$) and water.
  • Observations: Blue solid turns into black Copper (II) Oxide solid.

Reaction 4: Re-dissolving with Sulfuric Acid

$CuO(s) + H_2SO_4(aq) \rightarrow CuSO_4(aq) + H_2O(l)$

  • Reaction Type: Double Replacement.
  • Compound Name in Solution: Copper (II) Sulfate.
  • Observations: Black solid dissolves; liquid turns sky blue.

Reaction 5: Reduction back to Metal

$CuSO_4(aq) + Zn(s) \rightarrow ZnSO_4(aq) + Cu(s)$

  • Reaction Type: Single Replacement.
  • Second Reaction: $Zn(s) + 2HCl(aq) \rightarrow ZnCl_2(aq) + H_2(g)$.
  • Gas Produced: Hydrogen gas ($H_2$).
  • What is removed by decanting? Zinc sulfate ($ZnSO_4$) and unreacted $HCl$.
  • Description of Recovered Copper: Reddish-orange, granular metallic solid.

POST-LAB QUANTITATIVE QUESTIONS

1. Give possible reasons why the percent recovery might be low.

Common causes include: Loss of product during decanting steps; incomplete reaction in the thermal decomposition stage; mechanical loss (solid sticking to beakers or stirring rods).

2. Give possible reasons why the percent recovery might be high (over 100%).

Common causes include: Incomplete drying of the final copper product (residual water or acetone); presence of unreacted zinc metal; contamination from other salts not fully washed away.

3. How many milliliters of 3.0 M NaOH are required to react with 0.50 g of $Cu^{2+}$ to form $Cu(OH)_2$?

Calculation:

  1. Moles of $Cu^{2+} = 0.50\text{ g} / 63.55\text{ g/mol} = 0.00787\text{ mol}$.
  2. Stoichiometry ($1 Cu : 2 NaOH$): $0.00787 \times 2 = 0.01574\text{ mol NaOH}$.
  3. Volume: $V = n / M = 0.01574\text{ mol} / 3.0\text{ M} = 0.00525\text{ L}$.
  4. Result: $5.25\text{ mL}$.

4. How much zinc is required to react with 0.50 g of $Cu^{2+}$ to form Cu metal? Is there excess?

Calculation:

  1. Moles of $Cu^{2+} = 0.00787\text{ mol}$.
  2. Stoichiometry ($1:1$ ratio): $0.00787\text{ mol Zn required}$.
  3. Mass: $0.00787 \times 65.38\text{ g/mol} = \mathbf{0.515\text{ g Zn}}$.

Is there excess? Yes. The procedure added $2.0\text{ g}$ of Zinc. The excess is dissolved by adding $HCl$.

YIELD CALCULATION

Initial mass: $0.5506\text{ g}$.

Final mass recovered: $0.5136\text{ g}$.

$$\text{Percent Yield} = \left( \frac{0.5136}{0.5506} \right) \times 100 = 93.28\% \text{}.$$
